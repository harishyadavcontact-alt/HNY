The transfer of risk and absence of skin in the game are key drivers of naïve interventionism.

### 1. Risk Transfer

Naïve interventionists “become antifragile at the expense of others by getting the upside (or gains) from volatility… and exposing others to the downside risks of losses or harm”. This is called the “malignant transfer of fragility and antifragility”.

### 2. The Agency Problem

This core problem involves the misalignment of interest between the agent (interventionist) and the principal (society/client). The interventionist follows a strategy that benefits him and “makes him antifragile at the expense (fragility) of the true owners or society”.

### 3. The Bob Rubin Trade

This is a classic payoff structure “where the benefits are visible (and rewarded with some compensation) and the detriment is rare (and unpunished owing to absence of skin in the game)”.

### 4. Moral Hazard

Since “a bonus system based on ‘performance’ exacerbates the problem”, and the benefits are visible, the interventionist is incentivized to act (acts of commission) rather than omit (acts of omission), leading to the “doctor who makes the surgery look indispensable” being rewarded over the one who practices restraint.

### 5. Lack of Learning

The transfer of risk “impedes learning”. Systems learn by filtering and removing parts, via negativa. Interventionistas do not learn because they are “not the victims of their mistakes”.

### D. The Expert Problem

**Expert problem:** Expert problems (in which the expert knows a lot but less than he thinks he does) often bring fragilities, and acceptance of ignorance the reverse.

Expert problems put you on the wrong side of asymmetry. Let us examine the point with respect to risk. When you are fragile you need to know a lot more than when you are antifragile. Conversely, when you think you know more than you do, you are fragile (to error).

### E. Connection to Naïve Rationalism and Iatrogenics

The sources demonstrate that naïve rationalism directly leads to naïve interventionism by providing the flawed confidence needed for people to interfere with systems they don’t truly understand:

1. **Overconfidence in Knowledge:** The fragilista, empowered by the Soviet-Harvard delusion, thinks he knows better than nature or tradition and “makes you engage in policies and actions, all artificial, in which the benefits are small and visible, and the side effects potentially severe and invisible”.

1. **Iatrogenics through Denial of Antifragility:** Naïve interventionism, born from this rationalist urge, is the source of harm, as it lies in the “denial of antifragility, and to the impression that we humans are so necessary to making things function”.

1. **Complexification and Compounding Errors:** The naïve rationalist often introduces complexity, believing it is sophisticated. However, in complex systems, complications lead to “multiplicative chains of unanticipated effects”. The urge to intervene leads to unforeseen consequences, followed by further interventions to correct the secondary effects, leading to an “explosive series of branching ‘unforeseen’ responses, each one worse than the preceding one”.

### F. A Counterpoint: Capitalism’s Inverse-Iatrogenic Effect

Perhaps the idea behind capitalism is an inverse-iatrogenic effect, the unintended-but-not-so-unintended consequences: the system facilitates the conversion of selfish aims (or, to be correct, not necessarily benevolent ones) at the individual level into beneficial results for the collective.
