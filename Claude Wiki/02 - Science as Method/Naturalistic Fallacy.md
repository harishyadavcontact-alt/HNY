# Naturalistic Fallacy

> The mistake of assuming that because something is "natural" (i.e., evolved, ancestral, or common in nature), it is therefore good, correct, or what we ought to do.

---

## Definition

The naturalistic fallacy (in evolutionary context): inferring *what ought to be* from *what is* in nature.

If humans evolved to be tribalistic — does that mean tribalism is good?
If males evolved to seek multiple mates — does that mean infidelity is acceptable?
If aging is natural — does that mean we shouldn't treat age-related disease?

All of these would be naturalistic fallacies.

---

## Why It Matters for This Framework

Heying and Weinstein explicitly acknowledge and guard against this:

> "Among the many benefits of first principles thinking is that it helps one avoid falling prey to the naturalistic fallacy — the idea that 'what is' in nature is 'what ought to be.' The framework that we present here is built to free us from these sorts of traps."
> — Heying & Weinstein

The evolutionary framework *describes* reality. It does not automatically *prescribe* behavior. Understanding why we have certain drives and instincts is not the same as endorsing them.

---

## The Correct Use

The evolutionary framework applied *without* the naturalistic fallacy:

1. **Describe:** This drive/behavior has an evolutionary basis in environment X
2. **Understand:** In environment X it was adaptive because of Y
3. **Evaluate:** Does environment X match our current environment? Does the drive serve us now?
4. **Choose:** Armed with understanding, make an intentional choice

Step 4 is the key: understanding our evolutionary inheritance gives us the ability to *choose* how to relate to it, rather than being unconsciously driven by it.
