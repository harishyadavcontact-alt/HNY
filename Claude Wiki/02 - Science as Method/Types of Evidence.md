# Types of Evidence

> Four categories of evidence used in evolutionary biology. Good inference requires multiple types — consilience across types is what makes a conclusion robust.

---

## Overview

| Type | What It Is | Time Scale | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Molecular Evidence]] | DNA, RNA, proteins | Deep — billions of years | Quantifiable, widely available |
| [[Morphological Evidence]] | Fossils, anatomy, body plans | Deep — millions of years | Visible, directly observable |
| [[Developmental Evidence]] | Embryonic development patterns | Intermediate | Highly conserved across lineages |
| [[Behavioral Evidence]] | Actions, social patterns, ecology | Fast — generations or less | Best for microevolution, ecology |

---

## The One-Type Trap

> "You can't have consilience between different kinds of evidence if you only have one kind of evidence. It's the kind of mistake that the reductionist, mechanistic, data-driven pseudo-scientific investigations that many are involved in now are making."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 1

Modern molecular biology often commits this error: using DNA sequences alone to infer evolutionary history, without checking against fossils, development, or behavior. The result is confident but sometimes incorrect phylogenies.

---

## The Complementary Problem

Each type is strong where others are weak:
- Molecular evidence fails when convergent sequences evolve independently
- Morphological evidence fails when unrelated species look similar (convergence)
- Behavioral evidence is too fast-changing for deep history but perfect for current ecology
- Developmental evidence provides a conservative, deep signal often missed by the others

---

## The Bear and Seal Skull Test

A bear skull and a seal skull look nearly identical. If you only use gross morphology, you can't tell them apart. But teeth (a specific morphological character + diet inference from behavior) reveals the difference immediately.

The lesson: know which type of evidence answers which question.
