# Behavioral Evidence

> The fastest-changing type of evolutionary evidence. A problem for deep history inference, but the most interesting evidence for understanding current ecology and microevolution.

---

## What It Is

Behavioral evidence refers to observed actions, social structures, and ecological patterns of organisms. Unlike fossils or DNA, behavior leaves almost no direct record — it must be observed directly or inferred from artifacts.

---

## The Paradox of Behavioral Evidence

Behavior changes rapidly — within a single generation, without requiring a reproductive event. This makes it:

**Problematic for [[Macroevolution]]:** Too noisy. Convergent behavior (same behavior evolved independently) creates false signals about ancestry. A behavior shared by two species might mean they're closely related — or it might mean they face the same ecological pressures.

**Invaluable for [[Microevolution]]:** Perfect signal. Fast-changing, convergent behavior tells you exactly what ecological pressures an organism is under *right now*. The things that are most aggravating for the macroevolutionist are most interesting for the microevolutionist.

> "Depending on what time scale you're interested in, the things that are most interesting and most aggravating are actually the opposite."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

---

## Application: The Usual Suspects

Behavioral evidence is the primary evidence for [[The Usual Suspects — Overview]]:
- Corvids using tools
- Dolphins using sponges
- Baboons displaying theory of mind
- Whales with cultural songs

These behavioral observations across unrelated lineages show [[Convergent Evolution]] of intelligence-related behaviors — evidence for the ecological demands of complex social life.
