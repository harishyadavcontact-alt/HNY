# Developmental Evidence

> Patterns in embryonic and larval development used to infer evolutionary relationships. One of the most conserved signals in all of biology.

---

## What It Is

Developmental evidence examines how organisms grow from fertilized egg to adult. Early developmental stages are remarkably conserved across distant lineages — fish embryos, reptile embryos, and mammal embryos (including humans) pass through strikingly similar stages.

**Strengths:**
- Highly conserved — developmental pathways resist change because most mutations are lethal early
- Independent of adult morphology — tells a different story than adult anatomy
- Connects molecular (gene expression) and morphological (body form) levels

**Weaknesses:**
- Less accessible than adult morphology
- Heterochrony (changes in developmental timing) can be mistaken for other relationships
- Requires laboratory or field observation of living specimens at multiple life stages

---

## Why It Is Conserved

Early developmental processes are deeply embedded. A mutation in a key developmental gene early in development is almost always fatal. So these pathways are protected from change by selection — they are essentially frozen in evolutionary time.

This means: if two distantly related species share the same developmental pathway, that pathway is very likely *ancestral* — genuinely shared, not convergently evolved.

---

## The Biogenetic Law (Cautionary)

Haeckel's "ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny" was an oversimplification — embryos do not literally replay their evolutionary history. But the underlying insight survives: developmental stages carry deep phylogenetic signal.
