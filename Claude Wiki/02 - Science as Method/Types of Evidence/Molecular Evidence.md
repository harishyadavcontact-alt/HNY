# Molecular Evidence

> DNA sequences, RNA, and protein structures used to infer evolutionary relationships. The dominant form of evidence in modern phylogenetics.

---

## What It Is

Molecular evidence compares genetic sequences across species to infer relatedness. The more similar two organisms' DNA, the more recently they shared a common ancestor.

**Strengths:**
- Quantifiable — distances can be measured precisely
- Available for almost all organisms
- Independent of morphology — can catch cases where bodies converged but genes diverged

**Weaknesses:**
- Convergent molecular evolution can occur — similar sequences can evolve independently
- Rate variation — not all genes change at the same rate
- Without other evidence types, can produce incorrect phylogenies

---

## The Reductionism Risk

Modern molecular phylogenetics sometimes treats DNA evidence as the only arbiter of evolutionary history. This is precisely the [[Consilience of Evidence|one-type-of-evidence mistake]] that Heying identifies:

> "You can't have consilience between different kinds of evidence if you only have one kind of evidence."

A phylogeny built on molecular data alone, without checking against fossils, developmental patterns, or behavior, is only as strong as the molecular signal — which can be wrong.

---

## In Practice

Molecular evidence is most powerful when it:
1. Agrees with [[Morphological Evidence]] from fossils
2. Is consistent with [[Developmental Evidence]] from embryology
3. Predicts patterns seen in [[Behavioral Evidence]]

When molecular evidence conflicts with other types, that conflict is a [[Paradox as Signal — X on the Map|paradox signal]] — something important is happening there.
