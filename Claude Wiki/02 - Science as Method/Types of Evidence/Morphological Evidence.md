# Morphological Evidence

> The study of body form — fossils, anatomical structures, skeletal features — to infer evolutionary relationships and history.

---

## What It Is

Morphology = form and structure of organisms. Morphological evidence includes:
- Fossil record (preserved body structures)
- Comparative anatomy (homologous structures across living species)
- Bone, teeth, and shell comparisons

**Strengths:**
- Directly observable
- Provides temporal data (fossils have ages)
- Long history of use — massive comparative database

**Weaknesses:**
- Convergent morphology misleads (dolphins and sharks look similar, are not closely related)
- Soft tissues rarely preserved in fossils
- Susceptible to same convergence problems as other evidence types

## The Bear and Seal Skull

Weinstein's mammalogy story illustrates morphological evidence perfectly:

A bear skull and a seal skull look virtually identical with the skin off. Gross morphology alone cannot distinguish them. But *specific* morphological evidence — teeth shape — tells you immediately: seal teeth are built for fish; bear teeth are built for omnivory.

**Lesson:** Which morphological character you examine matters as much as using morphology at all. You need to know where to look.

---

## Homology vs Analogy

- **Homologous structures:** Same ancestral origin, different functions (human arm, bat wing, whale flipper — all are the same bones, modified)
- **Analogous structures:** Different origins, similar function (bird wing and insect wing — superficially similar but independently evolved)

Homology is evidence of ancestry. Analogy is evidence of [[Convergent Evolution]]. Confusing them is one of the most common errors in morphological analysis.
