# Wonder as Scientific Stance

> Philosophy begins in wonder. So does science. The wide-eyed, defamiliarizing gaze that asks "how does that work?" is not naive — it is the prerequisite for genuine discovery.

---

## The Claim

> "It seems to me that the wide-eyed, defamiliarizing, wondering looking at the world is a stance, an effort and endeavor that profoundly unites all the forms of human inquiry — scientific, humanistic, artistic and so on."
> — Stephen Blackwood, Sophia Lecture 3

Wonder is not pre-scientific. It is the *origin* of science, and it remains necessary throughout. The person who has lost wonder has also lost the ability to notice what doesn't fit their model.

---

## Aristotle and Plato

Both Aristotle and Plato: "philosophy begins in wonder." This is not metaphor. The question "how does that work?" — the amazement at the workings of the world — is the engine of inquiry.

The modern separation of science from wonder is false and damaging. It produces scientists who are technically competent but cannot ask the right questions.

---

## Hopkins and Huxley Together

Two approaches to the same insight:

**Hopkins** (poet): "What you look hard at seems to look hard at you." Deep attention is reciprocal. Nature reveals itself to those who look with genuine curiosity.

**Huxley** (scientist): "Sit down before a fact as a little child." The child's wonder — not corrupted by premature model-building — is the ideal scientific stance.

Together: wonder = openness to what is actually there, not just what you expected.

---

## Against Scientism

Scientism — the attitude that science has already figured everything out, or will soon, and that wonder is naive — is the enemy of actual science.

> "I would like to take back science for the courageous and the creative and the curious."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 1
