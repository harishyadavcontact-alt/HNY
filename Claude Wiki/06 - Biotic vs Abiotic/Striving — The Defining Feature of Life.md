# Striving — The Defining Feature of Life

> Every living thing strives toward an objective it does not understand. This is what separates biotic from abiotic matter.

---

## The Observation

> "What that animal is doing is it is striving. It is striving to do something it doesn't understand. It probably thinks it's striving just to break into that clam. But it is actually striving to do something else. It is built to strive towards an objective that you do not need to know in order to accomplish it."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

The sea otter breaking into the clam: it doesn't know it is trying to propagate its genes. It knows it is hungry and the clam looks like food. The objective — genetic propagation — is built into the machinery, not accessible to the organism's consciousness.

---

## The Salmon Example

Water flows downhill. A salmon is mostly water. The salmon flows uphill.

The salmon doesn't understand why it swims upstream. It does not know about spawning grounds, egg fertilization, or genetic lineages. It strives because evolution built striving into it — the ancestors that didn't strive uphill left no descendants.

---

## The Contrast: Abiotic Striving

Gold accumulates protons. Spiral galaxies accumulate stars. These look like striving — and they are the precursor to it. But they do not *strive* in the biological sense. There is no accumulated adaptive information. Each atom of gold is doing what its physics dictates, not what its ancestors learned.

Biological striving is different: it is shaped by [[Heredity + Selection = Adaptation|millions of generations of selection]], compressed into instinct, drive, and behavior.

---

## Implications

- Organisms do not need to understand the ultimate reason for their behavior to execute it perfectly
- Consciousness is not required for effective striving
- Understanding *what* an organism is actually striving toward (lodging genes into the future) reveals why apparently irrational behaviors are rational at the lineage level
