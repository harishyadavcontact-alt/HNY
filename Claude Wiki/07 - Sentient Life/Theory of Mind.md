# Theory of Mind

> The ability to understand that other beings have mental states — beliefs, desires, knowledge — that differ from your own. Long thought to be uniquely human. It is not.

---

## Definition

Theory of mind (ToM): the capacity to attribute mental states (beliefs, intentions, desires, emotions, knowledge) to oneself and to others, and to understand that others' mental states may differ from your own.

Classic test: the "false belief task." Can you understand that another person believes something *false* that you know to be true? If yes, you have ToM.

---

## The Baboon Example

> "These baboons have theory of mind. They have the ability to discern that what they think is true about something is not necessarily what some other baboon thinks is true about something."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

Baboons can model the beliefs of other baboons. They can recognize that another individual has a *false* belief and act accordingly — exploiting or accounting for that false belief in their social behavior.

---

## Why It Matters

Theory of mind is a prerequisite for:
- Complex social manipulation and cooperation
- Teaching (you must understand what the student doesn't know)
- Language (you model what the listener knows and doesn't know)
- Empathy
- Deception

If baboons have it, it is not uniquely human. It is a convergent solution to the demands of complex social life. It appears in the [[The Usual Suspects — Overview|usual suspects]] across multiple lineages.

---

## The Convergence Argument

The fact that ToM appears in:
- Great apes
- Baboons
- Corvids (ravens, jays)
- Dolphins
- Elephants

...all independently... tells you this is not an accident of ancestry. It is what complex sociality *demands*. It is a [[Convergent Evolution|convergently evolved]] solution.
