**Shape the World**

Rare and extreme events, known as **tail events**, lie at the outer edges of probability distributions and have the power to **reshape economies, societies, and industries**. These events, whether catastrophic or transformative, disrupt norms and create lasting impacts.

> [!important]
> 
> **Tail Weight (Heavy vs. Light Tails)**
> 
> - **Heavy-Tailed Distributions**
>     
>     - Have a higher probability of extreme events occurring (e.g., power laws, Pareto distributions).
>     
>     - **Example:** Wealth distribution, where a few individuals control the majority of resources.
>     
> 
> - **Light-Tailed Distributions**
>     
>     - Make extreme events highly unlikely (e.g., normal distributions).
>     
>     - **Example:** Human height distribution—finding someone above 8 feet tall is extremely rare.
>     

> [!important]
> 
> **Tail Size**
> 
> - **Small Tail**
>     
>     - Extreme events are very rare, and their probability declines rapidly.
>     
>     - **Example:** Normal (Gaussian) distribution.
>     
>     - **Real-world Example:** Human height—people over 8 feet tall are extraordinarily rare.
>     
> 
> - **Long Tail**
>     
>     - Rare events continue to occur, though less frequently, often seen in power law distributions.
>     
>     - **Example:** E-commerce—Amazon sells a few bestsellers but also numerous niche products.
>     
>     - **Example:** Music streaming—Spotify has a few hits but millions of niche songs with smaller audiences.
>     
> 
> - **Thin Tail**
>     
>     - Extreme events are exponentially rare, making their occurrence highly unlikely.
>     
>     - **Example:** Measurement errors in physics, where extreme deviations are almost nonexistent.
>     
> 
> - **Black Swan Event**
>     
>     - Extreme, unpredictable, and high-impact events that originate from fat-tailed distributions.
>     
>     - **Examples:** The 2008 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic.
>     
>     - **Significance:** They challenge existing risk models and require adaptive strategies.
>     

> [!important]
> 
> - **Left Tail**
>     
>     - The **left tail** of a distribution refers to the portion of the curve that is on the left side of the mean or median.
>     
>     - It represents the extreme low values or outliers that are far below the center of the distribution.
>     
>     - In many distributions, especially normal distributions, the left tail tends to be symmetric to the right tail. However, it can also show skewness if there are more values concentrated in the lower end.
>     
>     - If the distribution has a long left tail, it suggests that there are relatively frequent extreme low values (e.g., in financial markets, extreme losses).
>     
> 
> - **Right Tail**
>     
>     - The **right tail** refers to the portion of the distribution that is on the right side of the mean or median.
>     
>     - It represents the extreme high values or outliers that are far above the center of the distribution.
>     
>     - If the distribution has a long right tail, it indicates that there are more extreme high values (e.g., high-income earners in a population or large stock price increases).
>     
>     - In a normal distribution, the right tail is often symmetric to the left tail.
>     

> [!important]
> 
> - **Fat Tail**
>     
>     - Extreme events are more frequent than normal distributions predict, commonly seen in finance and wealth distribution.
>     
>     - **Example:** Stock market crashes, where sudden extreme losses happen more often than expected.
>     
>     - **Example:** A few people holding most of the world’s wealth.
