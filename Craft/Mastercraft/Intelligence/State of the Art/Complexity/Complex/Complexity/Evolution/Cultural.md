# Cultural

> Relating to culture — the shared, transmitted, heritable information layer that exists above genetics and shapes behavior across a population. Culture is the second inheritance system humans run on.

---

## What "Cultural" Means in the Evolutionary Framework

When something is described as "cultural" in the evolutionary sense, it means:
- **Transmitted** — passed between individuals or generations, not encoded in DNA
- **Shared** — held in common across a population
- **Heritable** — can be passed on with some fidelity

Cultural inheritance is distinct from genetic inheritance but operates by the same core logic: information is transmitted, some variants persist better than others, and the population changes over time.

---

## Cultural Transmission vs Genetic Transmission

| Feature | Genetic | Cultural |
|---|---|---|
| Medium | DNA | Behavior, language, artifact, ritual |
| Transmission | Parent → offspring only | Horizontal, diagonal, vertical |
| Speed of change | Generational | Can change within a generation |
| Mutation rate | Low | High |
| Fidelity | Very high | Variable |

Cultural transmission can move between non-relatives, across generations, and even between species. It is faster and more flexible than genetic transmission — which makes it powerful in novel environments and potentially unstable in stable ones.

---

## Cultural Evolution

Cultures evolve. Variants arise (innovations, errors in transmission), some variants spread while others don't, and populations change over time. This is evolution by cultural selection rather than natural selection.

The key difference: cultural evolution can happen far faster than genetic evolution, and cultural traits can be intentionally designed or modified — unlike genetic mutations, which are random.

This gives humans a second gear: when genetic evolution is too slow to keep pace with environmental change, cultural evolution can fill the gap.

## The Sponging Dolphins Example

A subset of female bottlenose dolphins in Shark Bay, Australia, wear marine sponges on their noses while foraging for fish on the seafloor. This protects them from abrasion and stings.

This behavior is not in their DNA. It is a **cultural innovation** — passed primarily from mother to offspring. It represents cultural transmission in a non-human species: a behavioral variant that emerged once, proved adaptive, and spread through cultural (not genetic) inheritance.

---

## Cumulative Culture

Cumulative culture is the ability to build upon the innovations of others — so that each generation inherits not just the current tool, but the history of improvements to it.

New Caledonian crows show this: their tool designs have improved over time, with more efficient versions replacing less efficient ones across generations. Archaeologists of crow tools find the same pattern as archaeologists of human stone tools.

Humans have this capacity to an extraordinary degree. Our technology, science, and institutions are all cumulative cultural achievements.

## Culture and Consciousness

[[Culture vs Consciousness|Culture]] is the stored, stable layer. [[Culture vs Consciousness|Consciousness]] is the generative, exploratory layer. They are not opponents — they are the two phases of the same cycle:

1. Consciousness generates new ideas
2. The useful ones get transmitted and become culture
3. Culture is the substrate on which the next generation's consciousness operates

Without culture, each generation starts from zero. Without consciousness, culture calcifies and cannot adapt.
