# Historical Science

> Macroevolution is a historical investigation. Its object is the past — relationships, lineages, origins. It cannot run controlled experiments on deep time. It uses consilience of evidence across independent lines to reconstruct what happened. This is history, done rigorously.

---

## The Two Scales of Evolutionary Biology

| Scale | Focus | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Macroevolution** | Origin of species; deep historical relationships; who is related to whom | Historical reconstruction via consilience of evidence |
| **Microevolution** | Small-scale change within lineages; behavior; adaptations | Experimental and observational; focuses on the quickly-changing |

Macroevolution asks: where did this organism come from? Who is its closest relative? What was the sequence of changes?

Microevolution asks: what is this organism doing now, and why? How does this behavior serve fitness?

> "Macroevolutionary inquiry seeks to understand historical relationships, whereas most of the stuff we're interested in — like how grief evolved — is at the smaller scales."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 1

---

## Why Macroevolution Is Different

You cannot run a controlled experiment on 65 million years of evolution. You cannot rewind the tape and watch dinosaurs become birds. Macroevolution is therefore **historical science** — like archaeology or forensics — in which you reconstruct the past from the evidence it left behind.

This makes it both powerful and vulnerable:
- Powerful: multiple independent lines of evidence can converge on the same answer
- Vulnerable: every conclusion is inference, and gestalt impressions regularly mislead

---

## The Core Tool: Characters

Macroevolutionary biologists use **characters** (not "characteristics" — the field insists on the technical term) to reconstruct history. Characters are the individual modifications that occurred in descent:

> "Characters are the individual things that change over time — the modifications that happen in that 'descent with modification.'"
> — Heather Heying

**Types of characters:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Molecular** | DNA sequence data | Base pair differences between genomes |
| **Morphological** | Physical structures | Skull shape, limb arrangement, tooth form |
| **Developmental** | Ontogenetic patterns | Embryological stages, developmental timing |
| **Behavioral** | Behavioral traits | Migration patterns, mating systems |
| **Symbolic** | Language and symbol evolution | Phylogenies of language families |

---

## Why One Line of Evidence Is Not Enough

The critical principle: **consilience of evidence**. You cannot reach reliable conclusions from a single character type. Molecular data alone, morphological data alone, behavioral data alone — each can mislead.

> "You can't have consilience between different kinds of evidence if you only have one kind of evidence. It's the kind of mistake that reductionist, mechanistic, data-driven investigations are making."
> — Heather Heying

Example: Gestalt similarity between seals and whales (both live in water, both streamlined) suggests they are closely related. They are not. Bears and seals are each other's closest relatives. Whales' closest extant relatives are hippos. The gestalt fails; the characters reveal.

---

## Willi Hennig's Contribution

A century after Darwin, German entomologist **Willi Hennig** formalized the insight that gestalt-level observation is insufficient. His approach — **cladistics** (though Heying doesn't use this term) — required biologists to examine specific characters rather than overall similarity.

Before Hennig: "It kind of looks like X, so it probably is X."
After Hennig: identify the specific characters that changed in each lineage, and trace their evolution precisely.

This discipline is why we know that birds are dinosaurs, that crocodiles are more closely related to birds than to lizards, and that seals are more closely related to bears than to whales — results that consistently contradict naive intuition.

---

## The Inductive Nature of Historical Science

Historical science proceeds inductively: observe patterns in the evidence, propose the most parsimonious historical reconstruction, test whether it predicts other evidence you haven't yet examined.

The arbiter, as always, is **predictive power over the long term**. A historical reconstruction that turns out to accurately predict what we find in new fossils, new genomic data, new behavioral studies — is probably right.
