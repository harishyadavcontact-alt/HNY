# Human Universals

> The things all humans do and have, across every culture, in every time and place. Not coincidences — these are the signature of our shared evolutionary heritage. They took hundreds of millions of years to build. Rapid change puts them at risk.

---

## The List

All humans have **language**.

We can tell self from other, and distinguish self as subject ("I cooked for her") from self as object ("she cooked for me"). We use **facial expressions** that are both general and nuanced — happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust, and contempt.

We don't just use tools; we use **tools to make more tools**. We live in or under **shelter**. We live in groups, usually with family, and adults are expected to help **socialize children**. Children observe elders and copy them. We also learn by trial and error.

We have **status**, governed by rules stemming from kinship, age, sex, and beyond. We have rules of succession and markers of hierarchy. We engage in **division of labor**.

**Reciprocity** is important — both positive (barn raising, gift exchange) and negative (retaliation for wrongs). We **trade**. We predict and plan for the future. We have **law and leaders**, though both may be situational or ephemeral.

We have **rituals, religious practice**, and standards of sexual modesty. We admire hospitality and generosity. We have an **aesthetic**, which we apply to our bodies, our hair, and our environment.

We know how to **dance**. We make **music**. We **play**.

---

## What the List Reveals

These universals were not invented yesterday. They trace back through hundreds of millions of years of evolutionary history:
- Language, tool use, and cultural transmission → deep primate heritage
- Facial expressions of emotion → mammalian heritage
- Social living, hierarchy, reciprocity → heritage shared with many social animals
- Play → shared with all organisms that have long childhoods
- Religious practice and ritual → lineage-level cultural investment (see [[Notre Dame Paradox]])

> "It took so long for us to become who we are today. If you look deeply into the history of life on our planet, you can see how these universals emerged over hundreds of millions of years, and once you understand this, you will see why change, especially rapid change, isn't always such a good thing."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## Universals vs Particulars

The universals are the invariant pattern. The particulars are the specific instantiations:
- **Universal**: All humans have language
- **Particular**: The specific languages differ across populations

- **Universal**: All humans have music
- **Particular**: The specific musical forms differ

The universals are what [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Complexity/Complex/Complexity/Evolution/WEIRD]] research misses — by studying only Western educated populations, it mistakes the particulars of WEIRD culture for universal human nature.

---

## The Continuity Argument

> "Humans have retained the vast majority of these innovations from our history, from brains and bone to agriculture and boats. We breathe air and generate heat. We have efficient hearts. We have limbs and hands and feet. We are dexterous, agile, and social. We walk upright. We have just a few children at a time, and those children learn from their elders and from each other."
> — Heying & Weinstein

The universals are not a cultural choice. They are our biological and cultural inheritance. They are what make us human. **Our differences are fascinating, but our similarities make us human.**

---

## Why Rapid Change Is Dangerous

[[Hyper-Novelty]] disrupts the alignment between our universal adaptations and our environment. The universals evolved for environments that no longer exist. When the environment changes faster than culture can track, the human universals become sources of mismatch rather than sources of flourishing.
