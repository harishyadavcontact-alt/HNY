# Learned vs Inherited Migration

> Two strategies for navigating the world. One is flexible and updates. The other is efficient and static. The difference in outcomes demonstrates exactly why plasticity exists — and what we lose when we run on inherited programming in a changed world.

---

## The Two Strategies

| Strategy | Mechanism | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Learned migration** | Young birds learn routes from parents/flock | Take optimal paths — most efficient given current winds, mountains, stopping points |
| **Inherited migration** | Young birds arrive with routes encoded at birth | Often take wildly inefficient paths — as if the world changed around a program that didn't update |

Both strategies work. In a stable environment, inherited migration is fine — efficient, low-cost, requires no learning period. But in a changed environment, inherited routes become tragic mismatches.

---

## Why This Matters

The bird with inherited migration isn't stupid or broken. It is running a program that worked — for an environment that no longer exists. It flies across mountains that weren't there when the route was encoded. It stops at lakes that dried up. It heads toward habitats that shifted.

This is the same dynamic Heying and Weinstein describe as the central human problem: we are exquisitely well-designed for an environment we no longer live in.

> "The bad news is that you are exquisitely well-designed for an environment that we don't live in."
> — Bret Weinstein

---

## What Learned Migration Demonstrates

The birds that learn migration routes from experienced flock members:
- Always take the currently optimal path
- Automatically adjust as geography changes (lakes dry, forests shift, mountains erode)
- Transmit updated routes to offspring

This is cultural transmission at work: not just passing information, but passing *updated* information. Each generation hands over a map of the current world, not a map of the ancestral world.

---

## The Trade-off

Learning takes time. It requires a long enough childhood to be taught. It requires surviving long enough with a flock to observe the full route. Inherited migration is cheaper and faster — but inflexible.

This is why the [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Complexity/Complex/Complexity/Evolution/Long childhood,|long childhood]] exists: plasticity is expensive and slow, but it produces organisms that can track a changing world.

---

## The Human Version

Humans are like learned-migration birds in one critical respect: our behavioral routes (culture) are passed down generation by generation. But they can become outdated — especially in a period of [[Hyper-Novelty]] — and when they do, running on inherited routes is exactly as dangerous as the bird flying to a lake that dried up 10,000 years ago.

The solution is not to abandon culture. It is to use [[Culture vs Consciousness|consciousness]] to update the map when the route is wrong.
