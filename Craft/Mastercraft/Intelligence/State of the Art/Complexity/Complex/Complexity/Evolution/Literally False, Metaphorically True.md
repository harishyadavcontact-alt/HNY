# Literally False, Metaphorically True

> The relationship between culture and factual accuracy. Cultural beliefs don't need to be literally true to be adaptive. They need to produce fitness-enhancing behavior when acted upon. This is not a flaw in culture — it is its design.

---

## The Definition

> "Culture we define as beliefs and practices that are shared and passed between members of a population. These beliefs are often literally false, metaphorically true — they result in increased fitness if one acts as if they are true, despite the fact that they are either inaccurate or unfalsifiable."
> — Heying & Weinstein

A culturally transmitted belief that is literally false but metaphorically true is one that:
- Does not accurately describe physical reality
- But, when acted upon, produces behavior that was adaptive in the ancestral environment
- And was successfully transmitted across generations precisely because it worked

---

## Why This Happens

Culture is compressed ancestral wisdom, not a compressed scientific textbook. The mechanisms that generated and transmitted cultural beliefs operated over many generations of behavioral testing — not logical analysis.

A tribe that learned "spirits live in the river, do not pollute it" may have had no understanding of pathogens or waterborne illness. The literal claim (spirits) is false. But the behavioral outcome (clean water) was fitness-enhancing. The belief survived because it worked.

---

## The Chesterton's Fence Problem

This is why Chesterton's Fence applies to cultural beliefs: removing a cultural practice because you can't see why it exists may destroy something whose adaptive function is invisible to you.

> "Removing functional organs because we can't tell what they're for is not wise."
> — Heying & Weinstein

Many cultural practices — food taboos, rituals, social prohibitions — likely encode real adaptive information in a form that persisted precisely because it was effective.

---

## The Naturalistic Fallacy Inversion

This concept is often confused with the [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Complexity/Complex/Complexity/Evolution/Naturalistic Fallacy]] — "if it's natural, it must be good." The relationship is different:
- Naturalistic Fallacy: what IS, therefore ought to be
- Literally False, Metaphorically True: what WORKS doesn't have to be literally accurate

The evolutionary argument is not that cultural beliefs are morally correct because they're ancestral. It's that discarding them without understanding them is dangerous.

---

## The Science-Culture Tension

Science and culture occupy different epistemic modes:
- **Science** demands literal truth, falsifiability, predictive power
- **Culture** demands fitness-enhancing behavior, transmission fidelity, stability

The mistake is evaluating culture by scientific criteria. A culture belief doesn't fail because it's unfalsifiable — it fails when it no longer produces fitness-enhancing behavior.

---

## The Modern Problem

[[Hyper-Novelty]] means many ancestrally-adaptive cultural beliefs now produce maladaptive behavior. The environments have changed faster than culture can track. In this situation, the right move is not to discard all culture — it is to use consciousness to identify which cultural beliefs have become maladaptive and need updating.
