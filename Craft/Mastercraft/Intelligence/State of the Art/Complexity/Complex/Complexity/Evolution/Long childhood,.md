# Long Childhood

> Childhood is when we learn. The longer the childhood, the more environment can shape the brain — this is plasticity. It is a universal prerequisite for sentience.

---

## The Core Principle

Long childhoods exist because **plasticity** — the ability to be shaped by experience — requires time. An organism that is fully wired at birth cannot learn much from its environment. It runs on pure instinct, which is adaptive for stable, predictable environments but brittle in novel ones.

> "Long childhoods are particularly important because childhood is when we learn. And there are a number of characters I'm going to be talking about here, traits of humans that are also found in other organisms that reveal how much learning and the biological term plasticity is important to being able to think broadly and really be sentient."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

---

## Zygotic vs. Plastic Organisms

Two types of organism:

| Type | Development | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Zygotic** (instructions at birth) | All programming present from egg/sperm union | Inflexible but efficient; ends up what it was destined to be |
| **Plastic** (long childhood) | Programming shaped by experience | Flexible; can be greater *or* worse than baseline based on experiences |

Most organisms are zygotic — frogs hatch knowing how to be frogs. The usual suspects are the exception: they need years of learning, play, and social immersion to become what they are.

---

## Play as the Mechanism

Wolf cubs at play are not wasting time — they are honing, exploring, and learning. Play is:
- Risk-taking in a safe context
- Social calibration (learning pack dynamics)
- Skill building without fatal stakes
- Cultural transmission (learning from elders what to do)

> "If you've got an organism with a long childhood, there's a very good chance that much of what they are was not there at birth. They are learning how to be — these wolves are learning how to be wolves."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

---

## Migration: Learned vs Inherited

A direct demonstration of the value of long childhoods:
- **Birds with long childhoods** that learn migration routes from parents → *always* take optimal paths (most efficient given winds, mountains, stopping points)
- **Birds with inherited migration** (no learning) → often take wildly inefficient paths, as if the world changed under them and they are still doing what ancestors did thousands of years ago

Learning allows not just flexibility but actual efficiency — routes can be updated to reflect current geography.

## Canary Song: Freedom in Youth, Rules in Adulthood

Young canaries: imitate what they hear *and* experiment — they bring in new things, some sounding terrible, some beautiful. This is consciousness-mode: exploration.

Adult canaries: their song calcifies. This is culture-mode: efficient execution of established patterns.

This maps directly to the [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Complexity/Complex/Complexity/Evolution/Campfires- Culture and Consciousness|Culture vs Consciousness]] oscillation at the individual developmental level.

---

## Nurture and the Ideas You Carry

You are as good as the ideas you carry, as good as the fundamentals you've been given to build on. This is why the intellectual environment of childhood matters so deeply — the quality of the cultural inheritance you receive during the plastic years shapes what you can become.
