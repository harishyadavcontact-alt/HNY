# Naturalistic Fallacy

> The mistake of concluding that because something is natural, it ought to be. Evolution describes what *is*. It does not prescribe what *should be*. Confusing the two leads to either sanctifying cruelty or dismissing evolutionary insight entirely.

---

## The Definition

An argument that concludes that if something is natural, it is therefore how things ought to be — effectively applying moral judgment to, or inferring it from, the natural world.

Closely related to, and for most non-philosophers interchangeable with:
- The **is-ought fallacy** (Hume's guillotine): you cannot derive an *ought* from an *is*
- The **appeal to nature fallacy**: "it's natural, therefore it's good"

---

## Why It Matters for Evolutionary Thinking

The evolutionary framework describes what organisms do and why. It does not tell us what humans should do. Confusing the two leads to dangerous errors in both directions:

**Naturalistic Fallacy in action:**
> "Humans evolved to be violent/hierarchical/sexually promiscuous — therefore those things are fine or inevitable."

This is wrong. Evolution explains the origin of traits. It does not justify them morally.

**The overcorrection:**
> "Evolution produced terrible things, so we should ignore evolutionary explanations."

This is also wrong. Understanding that hunger is adaptive doesn't mean we should let people starve.

---

## The Evolutionary Toolkit Is Descriptive, Not Prescriptive

> "We have shared an evolutionary tool kit with which to understand the human condition, not to justify it."
> — Heying & Weinstein

The evolutionary framework is a diagnostic tool. It tells you:
- Why a behavior exists
- What environment it was adapted to
- Whether it is currently adaptive or maladaptive

It does not tell you whether to act on it.

---

## The Hyper-Novelty Dimension

An unregulated market tends to embody the naturalistic fallacy: it feeds us what we "naturally" want — short-term gratification, overindulgence, status signaling — without asking whether those wants are still adaptive or good for us.

> "A market that is unregulated will tend to embody the naturalistic fallacy — the mistaken idea that 'what is' in nature is 'what ought' to be."
> — Heying & Weinstein

Just because a behavior is built into our evolutionary heritage does not mean we should act on it uncritically in a hyper-novel world.

---

## The Corrective

Use evolutionary understanding to:
1. Diagnose why a behavior exists
2. Identify whether the ancestral environment for which it was adaptive still exists
3. Decide consciously whether to act on it, modify it, or resist it

The evolutionary framework is your map. It does not navigate for you.
