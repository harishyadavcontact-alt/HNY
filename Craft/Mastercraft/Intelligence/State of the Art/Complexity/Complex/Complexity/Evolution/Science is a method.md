# Science Is a Method

> Science is not a credential, a consensus, or a body of facts. It is a method — one that oscillates between induction and deduction. It only works if you adhere to the philosophy. And we have largely forgotten how to do it.

---

## The Definition

> "Science is the building of useful models. And it only works if you adhere to the underlying philosophy. Science is a method. Doesn't require you to have a degree. It doesn't require you to have a grant. Doesn't require you to be anything other than curious and ready to stick to the method."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

Science oscillates between induction and deduction: we observe patterns, propose explanations, and test them to see how well they predict things we do not yet know. Good models achieve three things:
1. **Predict more** than what came before
2. **Assume less** (parsimony)
3. **Fit with one another** — merging into a seamless whole (consilience)

---

## The Ultimate Goal

We seek a single, consistent explanation of the observable universe that:
- Has no gaps
- Takes nothing on faith
- Rigorously describes every pattern at every scale

This goal almost certainly cannot be fully attained. But there is every indication it can be approached.

---

## The Hierarchy of Complexity

Reality has layers, and predictability decreases as complexity increases:

```
Physics (least complex, most predictable) — near "theory of everything"
Chemistry
Biology — processes inside simple cells not fully understood
Organisms — mystery compounds
Social animals — neurological feedbacks investigate the world
Humans — brimming with paradoxes born of what makes us distinct
```

The complicated produces models. The complex produces mysteries.

---

## The Arbiter: Predictive Power

> "The arbiter of what is true is one thing and one thing only. It is predictive power over the long term. It is not about your credentials. It's not about whether or not the idea has been peer-reviewed. It's not above all else about consensus."
> — Bret Weinstein

Every great idea starts with a minority of one — one person who gets it first and is against the consensus. If you rule that person out because they violate consensus, you have ended science.

---

## What Science Is NOT

- Data collection alone ("just repeated observation")
- Consensus ("an invented substitute for truth")
- Credential-based authority
- Peer review as arbiter

> "We have universities filled with scientists and we are wasting all of this resource on them because they think they've evolved past the method."

---

## The Cryptic Dark Age

We appear to live in an age of scientific enlightenment — more papers published than ever. But the rate of *profound discoveries* is not keeping pace. In evolutionary biology, no major breakthrough since 1976 (The Selfish Gene as synthesis). Not because there is nothing to discover — because we have forgotten how to discover.

Ultimately, in this book and with these models, we seek a single, consistent explanation of the observable universe that has no gaps, takes nothing on faith, and rigorously describes every pattern at every scale. This goal almost certainly cannot be attained, but there is every indication that it can be approached. Though we may glimpse this end point from our modern perch, we are a long way from reaching the limits of what can be known.

That said, we are much closer to the goal in some areas than in others. In physics we seem tantalizingly near a “theory of everything,” 1 which really means a complete model of the least complex, most fundamental layer of explanation. As we move up in complexity, things become less and less predictable. Near the top of the stack we reach biology, where processes inside even the simplest living cells are nowhere near fully understood. Things only get more complex from there. As cells begin to function in coordinated ways, becoming organisms made up of distinct tissues, the degree of mystery compounds. The unpredictability jumps again in animals, governed by sophisticated neurological feedbacks that themselves investigate and predict the world, and once again as animals become social and begin to pool their understanding and divide their labor. Nowhere are we more regularly stumped than we are in understanding ourselves. We Homo sapiens are brimming over with profound mysteries—surrounded by paradoxes born of the very things that make us distinct from the rest of the biota.
