# Adaptive Landscape

> A metaphorical framework for conceptualizing how selection and adaptation operate. Introduced by Sewall Wright (1932). Organisms exist at points on a multi-dimensional landscape of fitness — local peaks of adaptation separated by valleys of low fitness. Evolution is uphill movement across this landscape.

---

## The Metaphor

Imagine a landscape with peaks (high fitness) and valleys (low fitness). Each point in the landscape represents a combination of trait values. Natural selection moves populations uphill — toward higher fitness.

Key insights the metaphor provides:
- **Local vs global optima**: selection can trap a population on a local peak, even if a higher peak exists elsewhere — because getting there requires crossing a valley of low fitness
- **Rugged landscapes**: in complex organisms, the landscape has many peaks and valleys, and finding the global optimum may be impossible
- **Shifting landscapes**: as environments change, the fitness landscape itself shifts — what was a peak can become a valley

---

## Practical Implications

The adaptive landscape metaphor explains why evolution often produces "good enough" rather than "optimal" solutions. A population can get stuck on a local peak because the path to a better peak requires passing through low-fitness territory that selection will not cross.

This is directly relevant to understanding cultural change:
- Existing cultural practices may represent local fitness peaks
- Moving to a better practice may require temporarily reducing fitness (crossing the valley)
- This is why [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Complexity/Complex/Complexity/Evolution/Niche Switching]] is hard — the path from one fitness peak to another goes through bad territory

---

## Sewall Wright

Sewall Wright (1889–1988) was an American geneticist and statistician who introduced the adaptive landscape concept in 1932. One of the architects of the Modern Synthesis of evolutionary biology.
