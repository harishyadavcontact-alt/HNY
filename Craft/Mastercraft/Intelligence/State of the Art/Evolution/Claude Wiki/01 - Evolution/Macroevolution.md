# Macroevolution

> Evolution at the level of species and above — the origin of new species, higher taxa, and body plans. Studied over geological time scales.

---

## What It Studies

Macroevolution asks: what are the historical relationships between species? How did major body plans arise? How do we infer ancestry when we cannot observe the past directly?

**Evidence types used:**
- [[Molecular Evidence]] (DNA, proteins)
- [[Morphological Evidence]] (fossils, anatomical structures)
- [[Developmental Evidence]] (embryonic development patterns)
- [[Behavioral Evidence]] (used cautiously — see below)

---

## The Challenge of Behavioral Evidence

Behavioral characters are a problem for macroevolution because behavior changes *rapidly* — within a generation, without a reproductive event. This creates noise.

> "Behavior changes rapidly. Behavior doesn't require a generation change, a reproductive event, to make a change in a lineage. So behavioral characters are a pain for those macroevolutionary biologists who are trying to infer history."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

This is the opposite of [[Microevolution]], where behavior is the *most interesting* thing to study.

## Convergent Evolution

One key challenge: when two species share a trait (e.g. snakes and worms both lack legs), this can be convergent — independently evolved — rather than inherited.
