# Microevolution

> Evolution at or below the species level — changes in allele frequencies within a population over shorter time scales. Where behavior becomes the most interesting evidence.

---

## What It Studies

Microevolution asks: what is this organism doing with its life, and why? What ecological pressures shaped its behavior, physiology, and social structure?

**Evidence types preferred:**
- [[Behavioral Evidence]] — things that change quickly and converge
- Physiological and morphological data at fine scale

---

## Why Behavior Is *Most* Interesting Here

In [[Macroevolution]], rapidly changing behavior creates noise — hard to use for inferring deep history.

In microevolution, rapidly changing behavior is the *signal*. The fact that dolphins and ravens both developed tool use independently is *fascinating* precisely because it happened fast and convergently. It tells you about ecological pressures, not ancestry.

> "Depending on what time scale you're interested in, the things that are most interesting and most aggravating are actually the opposite."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

---

## Convergence as Evidence

In microevolution, convergent traits (traits evolved independently across lineages) are highly informative. They tell you: this is what it takes to survive in this ecological niche. Leglessness in both snakes and worms tells you about their ecology, not their ancestry.
