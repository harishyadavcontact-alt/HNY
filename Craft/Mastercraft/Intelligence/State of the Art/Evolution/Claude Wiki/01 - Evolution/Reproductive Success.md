# Reproductive Success

> The number of viable offspring an organism produces. Useful proxy for fitness, but not the same thing.

---

## Why Biologists Settled on It

Fitness is hard to measure directly. Biologists needed something observable. Reproductive success (how many offspring survive to reproduce) is measurable. It correlates with fitness ~95% of the time, produces clean data, and enabled decades of productive evolutionary research.

> "Biologists having physics and chemistry envy tried to figure out how they could measure fitness. What they settled on was actually not too bad. They settled on reproductive success and they got really good results. Which is where the tragedy happens. When you get really good results, you start to think that was the right move."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

---

## Where It Fails

The remaining 5% is critical. Key failures:

**Sterile worker ants:** Personal reproductive success = 0. But the colony — carrying their genes via the queen — is maximally successful. A fitness metric that calls the worker ant a failure is broken.

**Trade-offs:** An organism that sacrifices current reproduction for future reproduction may score low on reproductive success in year one and high in year ten. The long-term gene propagation is not captured.

**[[Lineage vs Individual|Lineage effects]]:** Helping your siblings reproduce is genetically equivalent to reproducing yourself. Reproductive success of one individual misses this.
