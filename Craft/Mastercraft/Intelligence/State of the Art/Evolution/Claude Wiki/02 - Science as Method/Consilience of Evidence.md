# Consilience of Evidence

> You cannot have consilience between different kinds of evidence if you only have one kind of evidence. Accurate inference requires multiple independent lines pointing to the same conclusion.

---

## The Core Principle

> "You can't have consilience between different kinds of evidence if you only have one kind of evidence. It's the kind of mistake that the reductionist, mechanistic, data-driven pseudo-scientific investigations that many are involved in now are making."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 1

Consilience (from E.O. Wilson's term, originally from Whewell): the convergence of independent lines of evidence toward a single conclusion. When molecular data, fossil data, developmental data, and behavioral data all point to the same evolutionary history — that is consilience. It cannot be coincidence.

---

## In Evolutionary Biology

The four types of evidence in evolutionary inference:

| Evidence Type | What It Captures | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| [[Molecular Evidence]] | DNA sequences, proteins | Deep history, quantifiable |
| [[Morphological Evidence]] | Fossils, anatomy | Deep history, visible |
| [[Developmental Evidence]] | Embryonic patterns | Conserved across lineages |
| [[Behavioral Evidence]] | Actions and social patterns | Current ecology, fast-changing |

No single type is sufficient. Relying on molecular evidence alone, for example, leads to incorrect conclusions about ancestral relationships — the same mistake as looking only at the surface of a bear skull vs a seal skull.

---

## In Science Generally

The principle extends beyond biology:
- A good model predicts across multiple domains
- If a model only explains things in one domain and fails in others, it is probably wrong or incomplete
- [[All True Narratives Must Reconcile]] — across cultures, disciplines, and methods

---

## The Bear and Seal Skull

Weinstein's mammalogy exam story: A bear skull and a seal skull look virtually identical with the skin off. You need to know where to look — the teeth (built for fish vs. meat). Surface similarity misleads. You need the right *type* of evidence.
