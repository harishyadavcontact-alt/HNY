# First Principles

> Assumptions that cannot be deduced from any other assumption. The foundation of all reasoning. Working from first principles is the most powerful path to truth.

---

## Definition

> "First principles are those assumptions that cannot be deduced from any other assumption. They are foundational (like axioms, in math), and so thinking from first principles is a powerful mechanism for deducing truth, and a worthy goal if you are interested in fact over fiction."
> — Heying & Weinstein, *A Hunter-Gatherer's Guide to the 21st Century*

In mathematics, axioms are the first principles — the statements you accept as given, from which all else is derived. If your axioms are wrong, everything built on them is wrong.

---

## Why It Matters

Most knowledge is second-hand: we accept models passed to us by authorities, textbooks, or consensus. This is efficient but fragile. When the foundations are wrong, the whole structure fails — and because you didn't build it yourself, you don't know where the cracks are.

First principles thinking forces you to:
1. Identify your actual assumptions
2. Test whether they hold
3. Build your model only from what survives scrutiny

---

## Application in the Book

Heying and Weinstein claim to build their framework "from first principles as much as possible." The first principle:

> Life evolves. Organisms are designed by natural selection to propagate their lineage into the future.

From this single principle, most of their conclusions flow — about food, sleep, sex, parenting, culture — without needing to appeal to tradition, authority, or consensus.

---

## The Naturalistic Fallacy Trap

One benefit of first principles thinking: it protects against the [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Evolution/Claude Wiki/02 - Science as Method/Naturalistic Fallacy]] — the mistake of assuming "what is in nature is what ought to be."

Understanding *why* we evolved a trait (first principles) is different from endorsing the trait. The framework describes reality; it does not automatically prescribe behavior.
