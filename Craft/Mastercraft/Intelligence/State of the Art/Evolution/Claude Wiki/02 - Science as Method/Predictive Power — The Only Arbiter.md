# Predictive Power — The Only Arbiter

> The one and only test of whether a scientific idea is true is its predictive power over the long term.

---

## The Principle

> "The arbiter of what is true is one thing and one thing only. It is predictive power over the long term. It is not about your credentials. It's not about whether or not the idea has been peer-reviewed. It's not above all else about consensus."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

A model is only as good as its predictions. Does it anticipate things we didn't already know? Does it still hold up ten years later? Across different domains?

---

## What It Is NOT

| False Arbiters | Why They Fail |
|---|---|
| Consensus | Every great idea starts as a minority of one |
| Credentials | Having a PhD doesn't make you right |
| Peer review | Peer review checks methodology, not truth |
| Data volume | More data ≠ better model |
| Statistical significance | p-values without a model are just noise |

---

## The Minority of One

> "Every great idea starts with a minority of one person who gets it first and is against the consensus. If you rule out that person who believes something alone because he's violating the consensus, you have ended science right there."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

Semmelweis (handwashing). Wegener (continental drift). Darwin (evolution). All started as minority of one. All proved right by long-term predictive power.

---

## Application

When evaluating any claim:
1. What does it predict?
2. Have those predictions been tested?
3. Did they hold up over years, across domains?
4. Does it reconcile with other well-supported models? (see [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Evolution/Claude Wiki/02 - Science as Method/Consilience of Evidence]])
