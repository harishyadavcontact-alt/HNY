# Properties of Sentient Life

> If there is sentient life elsewhere in the universe, we can predict what it will be like. The properties are universal — consequences of the demands of complex cognition.

---

## The Argument

Heather Heying's thought experiment: if you believe there is sentient life elsewhere in the universe, you can infer things about it from first principles. Not what it looks like — that is a [[Particulars|particular]]. But what pressures it operates under — those are [[Craft/Mastercraft/Intelligence/State of the Art/Evolution/Claude Wiki/03 - Universals vs Particulars/Universals|universals]].

> "Consider what are the things you think are true of humans that are likely to need to be true of other forms of sentient life."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 3

---

## The Universal Properties

| Property | Why Universal |
|---|---|
| **Long-lived** relative to other life on their planet | Complex cognition requires years to develop; you cannot grow a brain like ours and die in months |
| **Social** — permanent aggregations | Intelligence evolved in and for complex social environments; solitary hyper-intelligence is probably not viable |
| **Long childhood** | [[Long Childhood → Plasticity|Plasticity requires time]]; the longer the childhood, the more the environment can shape the brain |
| **[[Cultural Transfer of Information]]** | Learning must be transmitted across generations; otherwise each generation starts from zero |
| **Encephalization** | Large brain relative to body — the metabolic cost of intelligence requires a body large enough to support it |

---

## The Particulars (What We Cannot Predict)

We cannot predict:
- What they look like
- How many limbs they have
- What sense organs dominate
- The specifics of their chemistry
- Their social structures in detail

These are particulars — specific instantiations of universal pressures in a specific environment.

---

## Why This Matters for Understanding Humans

The same logic applies to understanding human uniqueness. We are not studying an exception — we are studying a particular instantiation of universal pressures. Understanding what is universal about sentience helps identify what is genuinely unique about *Homo sapiens*.
