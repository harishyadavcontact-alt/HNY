Overton Window in the Context of Gerdian Mimetics  
Gerdian Mimetics (a framework based on mimetic theory) focuses on how ideas spread, evolve, and become dominant through imitation and social reinforcement. When applying the Overton Window to Gerdian Mimetics, the key insight is that shifts in societal norms occur through memetic propagation—ideas moving from fringe to mainstream as they are adopted and imitated by influential groups.  
How Ideas Shift in the Overton Window via Mimetics  
1. Introduction (Unthinkable) – A new idea is introduced, often on the fringes. At this stage, it is seen as radical or taboo. In memetic terms, only a small minority dares to engage with it.  
  
2. Controversial (Radical) – Some influencers or early adopters begin discussing the idea, often in a negative or cautionary tone. This increases its exposure, even if through criticism.  
  
3. Acceptable (Sensible) – The idea gains traction, as more people imitate its supporters. A growing number of figures in media, academia, or politics start engaging with it seriously.  
  
4. Mainstream (Popular) – The idea is widely discussed and starts influencing policies, cultural norms, or social behavior.  
  
5. Policy (Implemented) – The idea is now part of the accepted framework, influencing laws, business practices, or social expectations.  
  
6. New Fringe (Reactionary Backlash) – A counter-movement might emerge, attempting to push back against the now-mainstream idea, restarting the cycle.  
  
  
Key Memetic Mechanisms That Move the Window  
Mimetic Desire – People imitate prestigious individuals or groups who adopt an idea, causing it to spread.  
Scapegoating & Polarization – Controversial ideas can gain traction when opponents overreact, drawing more attention and curiosity.  
Humor & Subversion – Memes, satire, and irony can help smuggle fringe ideas into mainstream discourse.  
Normalization through Repetition – The more an idea is discussed, even negatively, the more familiar it becomes, making it feel less radical.  
  
Example: Cryptocurrency Adoption  
1. Unthinkable – In the early 2010s, Bitcoin was seen as a tool for criminals and tech nerds.  
  
2. Radical – Some libertarians and tech enthusiasts started advocating for it.  
  
3. Sensible – Mainstream investors began acknowledging its potential.  
  
4. Popular – Companies and banks started integrating blockchain.  
  
5. Implemented – Governments create regulations or their own digital currencies.  
  
6. New Fringe – Traditionalists and regulators push back against decentralization.
