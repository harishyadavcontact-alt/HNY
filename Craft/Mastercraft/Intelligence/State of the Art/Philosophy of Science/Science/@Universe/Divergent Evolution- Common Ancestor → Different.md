**Definition:** Related species evolve different traits as they adapt to different niches from a shared ancestor

**Mechanism:** Populations of a common ancestor face different selection pressures in different environments

**Result:** Homologous structures (same underlying anatomy, different functions) and speciation

**Examples:**

- **Tetrapod forelimbs (the classic case):** Bat wings, bird wings, whale flippers, human arms all share the one-two-many bone structure (humerus-radius/ulna-carpals) because they inherited it from a common tetrapod ancestor, but each adapted it to radically different functions

- **Lamprey gill arches → Shark jaws + gills → Mammal jaws + middle ear bones:** Same structure, different instantiation—the first/second gill arch of an ancient vertebrate became your ability to chew and hear high frequencies

- **Darwin's finches:** One ancestral finch species arrived in the Galápagos and diverged into multiple species with different beak shapes adapted to different food sources

- **Dog, wolf, fox, coyote:** All diverged from a common canid ancestor

**Why divergence matters:** Homology reveals ancestry. It's the evidence we use to reconstruct evolutionary history and understand who is related to whom.
