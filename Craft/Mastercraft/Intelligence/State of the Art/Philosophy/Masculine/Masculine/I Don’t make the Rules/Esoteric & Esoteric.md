- The Dangers of Hidden Truths
    
    The philosopher Leo Strauss sought a middle path between Locke's deliberate "self-stultification" and Schmitt's embrace of violence. If a single word could summarize Strauss's core theme, it would be caution. He believed that the great thinkers of the past understood the deep problems of politics far better than we do, but they concealed their most profound insights to protect both themselves and society.
    
    ## 4.1 The Core Idea: Reading Between the Lines
    
    Strauss argued that the great philosophers practiced an "esoteric" style of writing. They presented one message on the surface (the exoteric) for the general public and government censors, while hiding their true, more dangerous meaning "between the lines" for a few careful and intelligent readers.
    
    He illustrates this with a thought experiment: imagine a historian in a totalitarian country who doubts the official state ideology. Publicly, he would have to praise it. But esoterically, he would place "three or four sentences in that terse and lively style which is apt to arrest the attention of young men who love to think," signaling his true meaning to those who know how to look for it.
    
    ## 4.2 On Human Nature: A Truth Too Dangerous to Share
    
    Unlike Locke, Strauss did not believe that human nature was unknowable. He was convinced that "there exists a truth about human nature, and that this truth can in principle be known." However, this truth is so problematic and disturbing that its open discussion poses a grave danger to social order. Strauss reminds us of Goethe's warning, delivered via the character of Faust, that those who were foolish enough to "reveal their feeling and their vision to the vulgar, have ever been crucified and burned."
    
    ## 4.3 On Politics & Violence: The Veil over Founding Crimes
    
    For Strauss, an account of politics focused only on the smooth functioning of government is dangerously incomplete. One must also confront the question of origins, which are almost always violent. The archetype for the founding of a city is the "murder of Remus by his brother Romulus." For Strauss, this violent archetype is not isolated to classical myth; its status as a foundational truth is powerfully reinforced by its surprising convergence with what the book of Genesis says about the founding of the first city in the history of the world.
    
    Even the United States, Strauss suggests, is not an exception. He alludes to a "mischievous interpretation of the Louisiana Purchase and of the fate of the Red Indians" as evidence that America's greatness may be due not only to its principles but also to its "occasional deviation from them."
    
    The lesson is that a just society cannot exist without a foundation that may contain injustice. However, society must "leave these sad exigencies covered with the veil with which they are justly covered."
    
    While Strauss urges that the founding violence of society remain veiled, René Girard's theory proposes that this violence is the central secret of all human culture—a secret that is now being dangerously unveiled.
