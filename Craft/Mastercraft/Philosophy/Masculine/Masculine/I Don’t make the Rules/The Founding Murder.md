Secret of the Scapegoat

René Girard was a French literary theorist whose work culminated in a sweeping, all-encompassing theory of human nature, culture, and violence. At its core is a single, powerful mechanism: the human tendency to imitate.

## The Core Idea: The Engine of Imitation

Girard's central concept is mimesis, or imitation. He views humans as "gigantic imitation machines." This is not just how we learn language and customs; crucially, it is also how we learn what to desire. When we see someone else desire an object, we begin to desire it too. This "acquisitive mimesis" is the engine that drives us into escalating rivalry and conflict as we compete for the same objects of desire.

## On Human Nature: The Mimetic Animal

According to Girard, human nature is defined by our unparalleled "aptitude for imitation." This is both our greatest strength—the foundation of all learning and culture—and our greatest weakness. Because our mimetic drive is so powerful, and because we lack the "instinctual brakes" found in other animals, it threatens to escalate into a "war of all against all" from which there is no escape.

## On Politics & Violence: The Founding Murder Unveiled

Girard offers a radical three-step theory for the origin of society and its relationship to violence:

1. **The Scapegoat Mechanism**: The primordial "war of all against all" does not end with a rational social contract. Instead, mimetic forces cause the conflict to converge, transforming the "war of all against all" into a "war of all against one." The community spontaneously gangs up on a single victim—the scapegoat—and murders them. This act of unanimous violence miraculously brings peace and unites the community.

1. **Myth as a Cover-Up**: This "founding murder" is the secret origin of all human culture, from ritual to monarchy. To preserve the peace it created, this foundational violence must be concealed. It is covered up by myth—a story told from the perspective of the persecutors that insists the victim was truly guilty and deserved to die, thereby sacralizing the violence.

1. **The Apocalypse (The Unveiling)**: The modern world, through the influence of the Judeo-Western revelation, has slowly come to know the truth: the scapegoat is innocent. This knowledge makes the old myths and rituals ineffective at containing violence. This "unveiling" (the original Greek meaning of apocalypse) dismantles the sacred barriers that once protected humanity from its own mimetic fury, risking the release of unlimited, desacralized violence, which Girard identifies with the modern phenomenon of "terrorism."
