> [!important]
> 
> - [Forensic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forensic_rhetoric) (also known as judicial)
>     
>     concerned with determining the truth or falseness of events that took place in the past and issues of guilt—for example, in a courtroom[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric\#cite_note-Aristotle-66): I.10–15  
>     • [**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative)
>     
> 
> - [Deliberative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative) (also known as political)
>     
>     concerned with determining whether or not particular actions should or should not be taken in the future—for example, making laws  
>     • [**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epideictic)
>     
> 
> - [Epideictic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epideictic) (also known as ceremonial)
>     
>     concerned with praise and blame, values, right and wrong, demonstrating beauty and skill in the present—for example, a eulogy or a wedding toast
>     
> 
> - **Ethos**
>     
>     1. **:** Establishing credibility and authority by presenting oneself as knowledgeable, trustworthy, and ethical. This can be done through citing sources, using expert opinions, and demonstrating expertise in the subject matter.  
>     •
>     
> 
> - **Pathos**
>     
>     1. **:** Appealing to the emotions of the audience to evoke sympathy, empathy, or other emotional responses. This can be achieved through storytelling, vivid imagery, and language that stirs feelings.  
>     •
>     
> 
> - **Logos**
>     
>     1. **:** Using logic, reasoning, and evidence to support arguments. This includes citing facts, statistics, and logical reasoning to make a persuasive case.
>     
> 
> - **Rhetorical Questions**
>     
>     1. **:** Asking questions not to elicit answers but to make a point or draw attention to a particular issue. Rhetorical questions can engage the audience and encourage them to think about the topic in a certain way.  
>     •
>     

> [!important] **Rhetorical Devices**
> 
> - **Repetition**
>     
>     1. **:** Repeating key phrases, words, or ideas to emphasize them and make them more memorable to the audience. Repetition can create a sense of rhythm and reinforce the speaker’s message.  
>     2.
>     
> 
> - **Parallelism**
>     
>     1. **:** Using parallel structure in sentences or phrases to create a sense of balance and symmetry. This can make the message more impactful and easier to understand.  
>     3.
>     
> 
> - **Antithesis**
>     
>     1. **:** Contrasting ideas or words within the same sentence to highlight the differences between them. This can create a dramatic effect and make the argument more persuasive.  
>     4.
>     
> 
> - **Analogies**
>     
>     1. **:** Drawing comparisons between two different things to explain a complex idea or persuade the audience. Analogies can make unfamiliar concepts more relatable and easier to understand.  
>     5.
>     
> 
> - **Hyperbole**
>     
>     1. **:** Using exaggeration or overstatement to make a point. Hyperbole can be used to emphasize a message or create a dramatic effect, but it should be used judiciously to avoid sounding insincere.  
>     6.
>     
> 
> - **Irony**
>     
>     1. **:** Using words to convey a meaning that is opposite to their literal meaning. Irony can be used to create humor, make a point, or highlight contradictions in a persuasive argument.  
>     •
>     
> 
> - **Rebuttal and Refutation**
>     
>     1. Anticipating and addressing counterarguments to strengthen your own argument.  
>     •
>     
> 
> - **Call to Action**
>     
>     1. **:** Concluding your message with a clear and compelling call to action to motivate the audience to take the desired action.
>
