• Public Opinion  
◦ Public opinion is how a nation’s population collectively views vital policies and evaluates political leaders. In a democracy, the people have the power to shape their government, so understanding public opinion is crucial.  
• PR  
◦ **Make It Sound Good:** Framing and presentation matter in politics.  
• Influence  
◦ Public opinion is highly reactive to current events, especially economic ones.  
◦ **Government:** Government actions influence the population.  
◦ **Private/Interest Groups:** Interest groups work to make money and shape opinions. Think tanks are sponsored to influence public opinion.  
◦ **Media (Internet/TV):** Media outlets control the news cycle, impacting public opinion. They can influence what’s covered and what’s not, using techniques like priming.  
• Political Ideology  
◦ Family, social groups, race, gender, education, and other factors contribute to political ideology. There are two broad ideological categories:  
◦ **Conservative:** These individuals value tradition, capitalism, family, community, nation, and liberty. They support a free-market economy.  
◦ **Liberal:** Liberals advocate for government intervention and emphasize equality.  
◦ Elections are sometimes seen as the least important part of politics.  
◦ **Adverse Selection:** A problem that arises when making a choice without having all the necessary information.

• Vote banks  
  
1. **Demographic Groups**: Vote banks are typically formed around specific demographic characteristics, such as religion, ethnicity, caste, language, region, or socioeconomic status. These characteristics can play a crucial role in shaping the political preferences of individuals within these groups.  
2. **Identity Politics**: Political parties often engage in identity politics to appeal to and mobilize vote banks. They may make promises or policies that cater to the interests or concerns of particular demographic groups, aiming to secure their loyalty and votes.  
3. **Historical Factors**: Historical factors, including social and political movements, can contribute to the formation of vote banks. For example, the civil rights movement in the United States played a role in mobilizing African American voters.  
4. **Electoral Strategy**: Parties strategically target vote banks by fielding candidates who belong to or can relate to these groups. Campaign messages and outreach efforts may also be tailored to resonate with the concerns of specific vote banks.  
5. **Geographical Concentration**: In some cases, vote banks are concentrated in specific geographic regions or constituencies. Parties may focus their efforts on these areas to maximize their electoral gains.  
6. **Coalition Politics**: In multi-party systems, vote banks can be crucial for forming coalitions. Parties may ally with other parties that have support from different vote banks to secure a broader electoral base.  
7. **Polarization**: Vote banks can contribute to political polarization by emphasizing identity-based issues and drawing clear lines between different groups. This can lead to intense competition for the support of these groups.  
8. **Vote Bank Politics**: The term “vote bank politics” is often used pejoratively to describe the practice of pandering to specific demographic groups for electoral gain, sometimes at the expense of broader policy considerations.  
9. **Challenges and Criticisms**: While vote banks are a common feature of democratic politics, they can also be criticized for reinforcing identity-based divisions and for encouraging parties to prioritize short-term electoral gains over long-term governance and policy solutions.  
10. **Changing Dynamics**: The composition and behavior of vote banks can evolve over time due to generational shifts, changing socioeconomic conditions, and political realignments. Parties must adapt to these changing dynamics to maintain support.

• oligarchs  
  
1. **Oligarch:** A member of a small, powerful, and wealthy group that holds significant influence over political, economic, or social affairs.  
2. **Plutocracy:** A system of government or society where power is held by the wealthy elite.  
3. **Wealth Disparity:** The significant difference in wealth distribution between a small wealthy class and the general population.  
4. **Influence Peddling:** The practice of using one’s wealth and connections to gain political or economic advantages.  
5. **Power Broker:** An influential person who plays a key role in shaping political or business decisions behind the scenes.  
6. **Crony Capitalism:** A system where business success is influenced by close relationships between business people and government officials.  
7. **Oligopoly:** A market structure in which a few large firms dominate and control the market for a particular product or service.  
8. **Clientelism:** The exchange of political or economic favors between the wealthy elite and politicians.  
9. **Nepotism:** The practice of favoring family members or close associates with positions of power or privilege.  
10. **Political Patronage:** Granting favors, contracts, or appointments to individuals or groups in exchange for support or loyalty.  
11. **Rent-Seeking:** The pursuit of economic gain by influencing government policies or regulations rather than by creating new wealth.  
12. **Concentration of Wealth:** The accumulation of a large portion of a country’s wealth in the hands of a small percentage of the population.  
13. **Tax Evasion:** Illegally avoiding paying taxes to the government, often used by wealthy individuals or corporations.  
14. **Offshore Accounts:** Financial accounts held in foreign countries for the purpose of avoiding taxes or legal obligations.  
15. **Lobbying:** The act of attempting to influence decisions made by government officials or legislators in favor of a particular interest.  
16. **Monopoly:** Complete control or ownership of a product or service within a specific market.  
17. **Elite Capture:** When a small group of powerful individuals or organizations manipulate policies to their advantage.  
18. **Dynasty:** A sequence of rulers, leaders, or influential individuals from the same family or group.  
19. **Expropriation:** The government’s seizure of privately owned assets or resources, often without fair compensation.  
20. **Conflict of Interest:** A situation in which a person or entity has competing interests or loyalties that could bias their decision-making.

• statesmanship  
  
1. **Statesman:** A skilled and respected political leader known for their wisdom, diplomacy, and ability to navigate complex issues.  
2. **Diplomacy:** The art of conducting negotiations and relations between countries to promote peace, resolve conflicts, and achieve common goals.  
3. **Foreign Policy:** A government’s strategy and approach to dealing with other countries and international issues.  
4. **Negotiation:** The process of reaching agreements or settlements through discussions and compromise.  
5. **International Relations:** The study of interactions between countries and the factors that influence their behavior on the global stage.  
6. **Peacekeeping:** Efforts to maintain peace and stability in regions or conflict zones through international intervention.  
7. **Global Governance:** Coordinated efforts among countries to address global challenges and issues.  
8. **Summit:** A high-level meeting between leaders of different countries to discuss important issues and make decisions.  
9. **Bipartisanship:** Cooperation and collaboration between members of different political parties.  
10. **Humanitarian Aid:** Assistance provided to alleviate suffering and address humanitarian crises, often involving food, medical supplies, and support.  
11. **Treaty:** A formal agreement between countries that outlines their mutual rights, responsibilities, and obligations.  
12. **Multilateral Diplomacy:** Negotiations involving multiple countries to address complex international issues.  
13. **Crisis Diplomacy:** Diplomatic efforts aimed at resolving urgent and critical situations, such as conflicts or disasters.  
14. **Soft Power:** The ability to influence others through attraction, culture, and positive relationships rather than coercion.  
15. **Foreign Aid:** Financial or material assistance provided by one country to another for development, relief, or other purposes.  
16. **Ambassador:** A diplomatic representative of one country to another, responsible for fostering relations and representing their government’s interests.  
17. **Protocol:** The established rules and etiquette governing formal diplomatic and official interactions.  
18. **Track II Diplomacy:** Informal, non-governmental efforts to promote dialogue and build relationships between countries.  
19. **Goodwill Ambassador:** An individual, often a public figure, appointed to promote and represent charitable or humanitarian causes.  
20. **Rapprochement:** The reestablishment of friendly relations between countries that had previously been estranged.  
• fascism  
  
1. **Fascism:** An authoritarian and nationalist political ideology characterized by dictatorial power, suppression of opposition, and often extreme nationalism.  
2. **Totalitarianism:** A system of government in which the state has centralized control over all aspects of public and private life.  
3. **Authoritarianism:** A form of government characterized by strong central control, limited political freedoms, and obedience to authority.  
4. **Militarism:** The belief in the importance of a strong military presence and a readiness to use military force.  
5. **Nationalism:** An extreme form of patriotism that often involves the belief in the superiority of one’s own nation and a desire for its dominance.  
6. **Censorship:** The suppression of information, media, or speech that is deemed politically, socially, or morally unacceptable.  
7. **Propaganda:** Biased or misleading information used to promote a particular political agenda or ideology.  
8. **Paramilitary:** Organizations that operate similarly to a military, often supporting and enforcing the policies of a ruling regime.  
9. **Cult of Personality:** The practice of promoting and glorifying a charismatic leader through propaganda and mass media.  
10. **Racial Purity:** A belief in maintaining the genetic and cultural homogeneity of a specific racial or ethnic group.  
11. **Eugenics:** The belief in improving the genetic quality of a human population through controlled breeding.  
12. **Scapegoating:** Blaming a particular group or individual for societal problems or issues.  
13. **Corporate State:** A system in which economic interests and corporate entities are closely aligned with the government.  
14. **Fascist Party:** An organization or political party that adheres to fascist principles and promotes its ideology.  
15. **Autarky:** A policy of economic self-sufficiency, often promoted by fascist regimes to reduce dependence on foreign trade.  
16. **Blackshirt:** Members of fascist paramilitary groups, often dressed in black uniforms.  
17. **Dictatorship:** A form of government in which a single leader holds absolute power and authority.  
18. **Führer:** A German term meaning “leader,” often associated with Adolf Hitler’s title in Nazi Germany.  
19. **Anti-Semitism:** Hostility or prejudice against Jewish people, often a central component of fascist ideology.  
20. **Corporate Nationalism:** A blend of corporate interests and nationalism, where private companies work in support of the nation’s goals.  
• Fascism  
  
1. Totalitarianism: Fascism advocates for a totalitarian state where the government exercises total control over all aspects of society.  
2. Authoritarianism: Fascist regimes are characterized by strong centralized authority and often ruled by a single leader.  
3. Nationalism: Fascism places a strong emphasis on the nation as a unified and superior entity, often promoting ethnocentrism and xenophobia.  
4. Militarism: Fascist ideologies often glorify war and militaristic values, advocating for a strong military and aggressive foreign policies.  
5. Corporatism: Fascist governments may promote a form of corporatism where interest groups are organized and controlled by the state to maintain social order.  
6. Anti-democracy: Fascism rejects democratic principles and often seeks to eliminate or severely limit political pluralism.  
7. One-Party Rule: Many fascist regimes are characterized by a single dominant political party that controls the government.  
8. Dictatorship: Fascist leaders often hold absolute power and can rule by decree, bypassing democratic institutions.  
9. Cult of Personality: Fascist leaders are often elevated to near-deity status, with a strong cult of personality built around them.  
10. Suppression of Dissent: Fascist governments typically suppress opposition and dissent through censorship, propaganda, and force.  
11. Racism: Some fascist ideologies promote ideas of racial or ethnic superiority, leading to discrimination and persecution of minority groups.  
12. Anti-communism: Fascism often opposes communism and leftist ideologies, viewing them as threats to the nation.  
13. Anti-Semitism: Not all fascist movements share this trait, but the Nazis, for example, promoted virulent anti-Semitism, leading to the Holocaust.  
14. Social Darwinism: Fascist ideologies may incorporate the belief in the survival of the fittest and the idea that conflict and competition are natural and necessary.  
15. Expansionism: Some fascist regimes pursue expansionist foreign policies, seeking to conquer or dominate other nations.  
16. Traditionalism: Fascism often promotes a return to traditional values and rejects modernism, secularism, and liberal ideas.  
17. Rejection of Individualism: Fascism places the collective interest of the nation above individual rights and freedoms.  
18. Propaganda: Fascist governments use propaganda extensively to shape public opinion and reinforce their ideology.  
19. Paramilitary Organizations: Fascist movements often have paramilitary groups or militias that are used for political intimidation and control.  
20. Economic Control: Fascist governments may control or heavily regulate the economy, often with an emphasis on autarky (economic self-sufficiency).
