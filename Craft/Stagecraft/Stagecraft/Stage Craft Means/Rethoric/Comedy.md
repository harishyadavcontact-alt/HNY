• Verbal Irony  
## The character intentionally says the opposite of what they mean.  
◦ Sarcasm  
Sarcasm is an ironic statement meant to mock or ridicule another person. The amount of cruelty can range from light-hearted joking to downright nasty.  
For example, let’s say you baked a pie that tasted horrible  
and said, “Wow, I’m such a good baker!”  
, **“Wow, you’re such a good baker!”**  
**“Did your parents have any children that lived?”**  
if no one is being insulted, it’s NOT sarcasm.  
Remember: all sarcasm is verbal irony, but not all verbal irony is sarcasm. There is constant confusion over irony vs. sarcasm but hopefully you understand the difference now.  
Since sarcasm is designed to insult, it can become a character trait for bullies, siblings, villains, or anyone with a sharp tongue.  
◦ Understatement  
Understatement is when you say something to  
intentionally downplay what you actually mean. . If verbal irony is saying the “opposite” of what you mean,  
understatement is simply saying “less” of what you mean.  
“This hamburger isn’t terrible!  
“I’m gonna make him an offer he can’t refuse”(negiote or kill him)  
“it’s just a scratch” when you arm has just been chopped off.  
This type of verbal irony can also be used to add a sense  
of urgency. In this iconic example from Jaws, Brody’s line  
(“You’re gonna need a bigger boat”).  
◦ Overstatement  
Sometimes you just can’t find the words to express how  
you feel. To match the depth or intensity of what you’re  
trying to communicate, you need something like  
overstatement.  
Overstatement is when you use language to exaggerate  
your intended meaning. These statements count as  
figurative language and are not meant to be taken literally.  
Also known as hyperbole, overstatement is used to  
emphasis the importance of your statement.  
“I have crossed oceans of time to find you.”  
◦ Socratic irony  
Socratic irony occurs when you feign ignorance in order  
to get someone to admit something. In other words,  
‘playing dumb’ to catch someone in a lie or to confess to  
something they wouldn’t otherwise concede. It is a **verbal chess match** that gives your opponent a false sense of security that lures them into a trap**“You can’t handle the truth”** instantly became a classic  
and quotable line but pay attention to the questions  
leading up to that moment.  
“Hey, I thought you weren’t supposed to eat anything for a couple of hours after you’ve had a crown put in?”  
If your character need to get answers from someone  
unwilling to give them, Socratic irony is certainly an  
option.  
Ignorance can be a clever trick to get what you  
want. In certain situations, giving someone the impression that you are not a threat will lower their guard. And that’s the time to strike!  
It’s also a great way to showcase the intelligence of a  
character but it’s also an opportunity to entertain the  
audience who know the truth.  
Eg- For example, when you get an “F” on your term paper and say, “Wow, I did a really good job on my term paper!”**Why writers use verbal irony**  
Verbal irony can be a powerful tool in writing dialogue. When a character says exactly what they mean, that can be uninteresting both for the actor delivering the lines and  
for the audience watching them. Interesting dialogue has nuance and subtext – and it leaves a little (or a lot) of room open for interpretation.  
This type of irony can be used for any number of situations, from comedy to romance to suspense. It can be used to add depth and complexity to what characters say.

that really ups the resale value.”

Comedic Devices  
• Irony  
Saying or doing the opposite of what is expected or intended, often for humorous effect.  
• Satire  
Using exaggeration, parody, or irony to expose and criticize foolishness or corruption in society.  
• Sarcasm  
Saying the opposite of what is meant, often in a mocking or derogatory way.  
• Wordplay  
Using puns, homophones, or other linguistic devices to create humor.  
• Hyperbole  
Exaggerating a statement or situation to create a humorous effect.  
• Misdirection  
Setting up an expectation or assumption and then subverting it in a surprising way for comedic effect.  
• Observational humor  
Commenting on everyday situations or common experiences in a way that highlights their absurdity or humor.  
• Physical comedy  
Using exaggerated gestures, facial expressions, or movements to create humor.  
• Parody  
Mimicking or imitating a particular style or work for comedic effect.  
• Roast  
◦ Know your subject  
Before writing any roast jokes, take the time to research and understand the person you’re roasting. Consider their accomplishments, quirks, and personality traits.  
◦ Be clever and witty  
Roast jokes should be clever and witty, not just mean-spirited. Avoid using tired or overused jokes and instead try to come up with something original and unexpected.  
◦ Use exaggeration  
Exaggeration is a common technique used in roasting. You can exaggerate a person’s flaws or quirks for comedic effect, but be careful not to be too cruel or hurtful.  
• Relatable  
Past shareing.  
Comedian’s ability to connect with their audience by presenting jokes or observations that are relatable to the audience’s own experiences or perspectives.  
If the audience can see themselves in the comedian’s material, they are more likely to feel invested in the performance and to find it funny.  
Relatability is based on shared experiences or feelings that many people can relate to. For example, a comedian might tell a joke about the frustrations of commuting to work,  
• Meta comedy  
It’s a type of comedic expression that either directed toward some other subject or openly directed toward itself intentionally alludes to the very person who is expressing the humor in a comedic fashion, or to some specific aspect of that same comedic expression.  
• Overstatement  
An overstatement—otherwise known as hyperbole—uses extreme exaggeration for humorous effect. It is usually found in an everyday figure of speech which over-emphasises a point that would normally be considered less significant. This inflates the importance of what is being said so it appears somewhat more ridiculous, for example: “I nearly died laughing.”  
• Understatement  
An understatement, you’ll be unsurprised to learn, does the opposite to an overstatement, in that it is used to underemphasise a point which you would normally expect to be more significant. It tends to get most of its humour through ironic context, with a famous example being the scene in which the Black Knight in Monty Python and the Holy Grail has each of his limbs cut off but says, “It’s just a flesh wound!”  
• Juxtaposition  
Juxtaposition finds its humour by placing two seemingly incongruous elements together in order to make an amusing comparison. Very often, it conveys a sense of irony by offering up seemingly contrary associations to humorous effect, as demonstrated by much of Ambrose’s Bierce’s work in The Devil’s Dictionary, including: “Love, noun. A temporary insanity curable by marriage.”  
• Repetition  
Repetition is one of the most vital ingredients in humour.  
From catchphrases to call-backs, it’s not easy for writers to master, but the general principle is if you repeat something often enough—a word or a phrase—eventually it will become funny. For example, if a character keeps being asked the same question by each person he or she meets in your story, over and over again, their eventual exasperation can be exploited for comic effect, all thanks to repetition.  
• Puns  
We all love a good pun, as I’m sure most tabloid journalists would agree, but in literary fiction it’s hard to use them in any other context than a deliberate joke. By making your character crack a joke to a friend, perhaps, or simply having them play on the similarity of words, there are some who may find levity through this device, like someone saying, “What’s it called when a duck’s afraid of spiders? Aquacknaphobia.”  
• Double Entendre  
As a kind of pun, of sorts, a _double entendre_ is a word or phrase which initially has a relatively innocent meaning but its ambiguity lends itself to a more _risqué_ interpretation. The resulting humour stems from sexual innuendo, insinuating some kind of lewd connotation which may or may not exist, for example: “If I said you had a beautiful body, would you hold it against me?”  
• Taboo  
Any subject which is deemed taboo, or socially frowned upon, is ripe for humour. Sex, death, religion, bodily functions—anything which makes people feel uncomfortable can be rendered humorous if you can highlight something disarming about a controversial topic and hit upon a truth people will recognise, in spite of how uneasy it makes them feel.
