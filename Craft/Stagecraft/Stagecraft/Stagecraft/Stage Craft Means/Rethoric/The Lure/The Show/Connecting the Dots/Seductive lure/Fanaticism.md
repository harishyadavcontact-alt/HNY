# Fanaticism

Fanaticism is a complex psychological phenomenon characterized by extreme and unwavering devotion to a particular belief, ideology, or cause. It often involves rigid thinking, intolerance of differing viewpoints, and a willingness to go to great lengths, including violence, to advance or protect one’s beliefs. Several psychological concepts can help explain the underlying factors that contribute to fanaticism:

1. Commodity Fetishism:  
    In the context of the entertainment industry, especially Hollywood, celebrities can be seen as commodities. They are often marketed and packaged like products, and their images are carefully crafted to appeal to the masses. The glamour and mystique surrounding celebrities can be seen as a form of commodity fetishism, where people attribute special qualities and significance to these individuals, often beyond their actual talents or contributions.

1. Fetishization (in a broader sense):  
    While a sexual fetish typically relates to specific objects or preferences, the concept of fetishization can be applied more broadly to the way people idealize or objectify celebrities. In some cases, people may become so enamored with a celebrity that they idealize them to an extreme degree, projecting unrealistic qualities onto them. This can lead to a form of fetishization in which the celebrity becomes an object of obsession.

1. Fantasy:  
    Fantasy, as discussed earlier, is the creation of imaginative scenarios or ideas. People often fantasize about being with or meeting their favorite celebrities, which can contribute to their idolization and the perception of celebrities as larger-than-life figures. These fantasies can be a part of celebrity worship, where individuals create idealized images of the stars in their minds.

1. **Cognitive Dissonance**
    
    Cognitive dissonance theory suggests that when individuals hold conflicting beliefs or values, they experience psychological discomfort. To alleviate this discomfort, they may double down on their existing beliefs, becoming more fanatical in their adherence to them.
    

1. **Confirmation Bias**
    
    People tend to seek out and interpret information in ways that confirm their existing beliefs while ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence. Fanatics are often especially prone to confirmation bias, which reinforces their preexisting convictions.
    

1. **Group Identity**
    
    Fanaticism frequently occurs within the context of group identity. Individuals may become fanatical in their support of a cause or ideology because it aligns with their sense of belonging to a particular group. The group provides a source of social reinforcement and validation.
    

1. **Socialization**
    
    The process of socialization, wherein individuals learn and internalize the values, norms, and beliefs of their social groups, can contribute to fanaticism. People may become fanatical when they are exposed to extremist ideas from an early age or when they are surrounded by like-minded individuals who reinforce their extreme views.
    

1. **Emotional Appeals**
    
    Emotions play a significant role in fanaticism. Fear, anger, and a sense of righteous indignation can be manipulated or exploited by charismatic leaders or extremist propaganda to inflame passions and drive individuals toward fanatical actions.
    

1. **Psychological Projection**
    
    Fanatics may project their own fears, insecurities, or shortcomings onto others, perceiving those who disagree with them as threats or enemies. This projection can further fuel their fanaticism and justify aggressive or hostile behavior.
    

1. **Authoritarianism**
    
    Some individuals have a predisposition toward authoritarianism, which makes them more susceptible to following strong, authoritarian leaders and adopting their extreme beliefs without question.
    

1. **Socialization and Groupthink**
    
    In group settings, fanatics can be further influenced by groupthink, a phenomenon in which the desire for conformity and consensus within a group leads to the suppression of dissenting views and the emergence of more extreme positions.
    

1. **Deindividuation**
    
    In certain situations, individuals may lose their sense of personal identity and responsibility, becoming part of a collective identity. This deindividuation can lead to behaviors that individuals might not engage in under normal circumstances, such as violence or acts of terrorism.
    

1. **Escalation of Commitment**
    
    Once individuals become fanatical about a belief or cause, they may be more likely to escalate their commitment to it. This can lead to increasingly extreme actions as they try to justify their initial investment of time, energy, or resources.
    

1. **Radicalization**
    
    The process of radicalization involves the gradual shift toward extremist beliefs and behaviors. It often involves exposure to extremist propaganda, online echo chambers, and recruitment efforts by extremist groups.
    

1. **Psychological Vulnerability**
    
    Some individuals are more psychologically vulnerable to manipulation and radicalization due to factors like low self-esteem, a sense of marginalization, or a need for a sense of purpose and belonging.
    

It’s important to note that not everyone who holds strong beliefs is a fanatic, and fanaticism exists on a continuum. Factors such as individual personality, life experiences, and the social and political context all contribute to the development and expression of fanaticism. Understanding these psychological concepts can help shed light on the motivations and behaviors of fanatics, but addressing fanaticism often requires a multifaceted approach that includes education, dialogue, and efforts to counter radicalization.

## Similar behaviors

The behavior of a fan with overwhelming enthusiasm for a given subject is differentiated from the behavior of a fanatic by the fanatic’s violation of prevailing [social norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_norm). Though the fan’s behavior may be judged as odd or [eccentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_\(behavior\)), it does not violate such norms.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fanaticism\#cite_note-6) A fanatic differs from a [crank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crank_\(person\)), in that a crank is defined as a person who holds a position or opinion which is so far from the norm as to appear ludicrous and/or probably wrong, such as a belief in a [Flat Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_Earth). In contrast, the subject of the fanatic’s obsession may be “normal”, such as an interest in religion or politics, except that the scale of the person’s involvement, devotion, or obsession with the activity or cause is abnormal or disproportionate to the average.[_[ambiguous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify)_]

1. **Fans:** “Fans” is a shortened form of “fanatics” and refers to individuals who have a strong enthusiasm, interest, or devotion to a particular person, group, or thing. Fans are characterized by their passionate support, dedication, and often deep knowledge of the subject of their fandom. They can be fans of sports teams, music bands, movie franchises, TV shows, books, video games, and more.

1. **Fanaticism:** Fanaticism is an extreme and often uncritical devotion or enthusiasm for a particular cause, belief, or interest. It goes beyond mere fandom and can involve obsession, irrational behavior, and a willingness to go to great lengths to support or defend the object of one’s fanaticism. Fanaticism can be problematic when it leads to harmful actions or intolerance of differing viewpoints.

1. **Fanbase:** A fanbase is the collective group of fans who share a common interest in a particular person, group, or thing. It represents the broader community of individuals who are fans of a specific entity. Fanbases can be large or small and often have their own subcultures, traditions, and ways of interacting with the object of their fandom.

1. **Fan Service:** Fan service, sometimes abbreviated as “fanservice,” refers to content or elements intentionally included in a work of fiction or entertainment to please or cater to the desires and preferences of fans. This can take various forms, including:
    
    - **Sexual Fan Service:** Including suggestive or provocative content (e.g., scantily clad characters) to appeal to fans’ sexual interests.
    
    - **Nostalgic Fan Service:** Referencing or revisiting elements from earlier installments or versions of a franchise to evoke nostalgia among long-time fans.
    
    - **Character-Centric Fan Service:** Focusing on specific characters or character pairings that fans have shown a strong interest in.
    
    - **Plot-Related Fan Service:** Incorporating storylines, events, or moments that fans have expressed a desire to see in the narrative.
    

1. **Ship (Short for “Relationship”):** To “ship” characters means to support or desire a romantic or emotional relationship between them. Fans often create “shipping” pairings, such as “Harry and Hermione” or “Mulder and Scully.”

1. **OTP (One True Pairing):** This term refers to a fan’s favorite or ideal romantic pairing within a particular fandom. An OTP is the ultimate preferred couple.

1. **Canon:** “Canon” refers to the officially recognized and accepted events, characters, and storylines within a particular work of fiction. Fanfiction and fanart can explore non-canon scenarios.

1. **Headcanon:** A “headcanon” is a personal interpretation or belief about certain aspects of a fictional work that is not explicitly addressed in the canon. It’s a fan’s individual perspective or understanding of the story.

1. **Fandom:** A “fandom” is the collective community of fans who share a common interest in a particular work, franchise, or subject. Fans often refer to themselves as being part of a specific fandom, such as the “Star Wars fandom” or “Marvel fandom.”

1. **Cosplay:** “Cosplay” is a combination of “costume” and “play.” It involves fans dressing up as characters from their favorite series, often with great attention to detail, and sometimes even acting in character at conventions and events.

1. **Con (Short for “Convention”):** A “con” is a gathering or event where fans of a particular interest, such as comic books, anime, or science fiction, come together to celebrate their passion, meet celebrities, attend panels, and more.

1. **Fanart:** “Fanart” refers to artwork created by fans based on characters, settings, or themes from their favorite media. It can include drawings, paintings, digital art, and more.

1. **Fanfiction:** “Fanfiction” is original fiction written by fans using characters and settings from existing works. It allows fans to explore alternate storylines, relationships, or scenarios.

1. **Canon Compliance:** When fan creations, such as fanfiction or fanart, strive to stay consistent with the established canon of the source material, they are said to be “canon compliant.”

1. **AU (Alternate Universe):** In fanfiction and fanart, an “AU” refers to a story or setting that deviates from the original canon, often exploring “what if” scenarios.

1. **Crossover:** A “crossover” occurs when characters or elements from different fictional universes are combined in a single work. For example, a crossover fanfiction might feature characters from two unrelated series interacting.

1. **Fangirl/Fanboy:** These terms are used to describe enthusiastic and dedicated fans, typically with a positive connotation. A “fangirl” is a female fan, and a “fanboy” is a male fan, though these terms can be used for fans of any gender.

1. **Spoiler:** Information about an upcoming plot point or development in a story that, if revealed prematurely, can “spoil” the surprise or suspense for others who haven’t experienced it yet.
