- Rights do not Exist
    
    He challenges opponents to demonstrate the physical reality of rights: "Can you taste a right? Touch it? Smell it?" Because rights lack these tangible properties, he categorizes them as "conceptual," "fictitious," and "completely made up nonsense." Those who believe in social progress operate within what he terms "socially constructed delusions."
    

- If rights qualify merely as social constructs, what justification exists for enforcing them through violence? The position self-contradicts at its foundation.

## The Definition: Entitlement Absent Duty

- "A right to me is an entitlement absent Duty." This formulation captures the essential logic—if one possesses a right to vote, own firearms, or speak freely, no corresponding obligation compels exercise of that right.

- Rights constitute "entitlements period," devoid of duties.

- While the secular world fixates upon rights, Wilson maintains that "duties are fundamental" to legitimate belief systems. He views rights as "made-up nonsense" precisely because they carry no inherent obligation.

- **Enhancement**: Secular rights discourse conceals duty evasion. Demand opponents enumerate their duties before discussing entitlements.

- **Against Axiomatic Rights**: When opponents such as Tim Pool argue that rights prove "intrinsic" or "axiomatic" for human survival, Wilson dismisses these as "just assertions" lacking an "epistemic Foundation."

- The Reduction of All Rights to Physical Force
    
    - Absent objective existence, rights serve merely as linguistic proxies for the capacity to deploy violence. Wilson's assertion proves unequivocal: "All rights are is force. Force are rights. Everything comes down to actual physical Force."
    
    - A right proves "only as good as who can enforce it." Consequently, when someone invokes a "human right," they summon a "force doctrine which is applied in maintaining that a person can and always will be allowed to do X." Wilson posits that "absent Force law has no bite"—if a rule exists without consequences for violation, it fails to qualify as law. Therefore, "enforcement always must be backed with physical violence for a law or for any type of right period."
    
    - The culminating reality: "At the end of every rule and law is a gun."
    
    - **Critical Stressor**: In the absence of police enforcement, does your "right to property" prevent the thief from taking your possessions? The answer remains unambiguous: No. Force wins.
    

  

- **Against Social Norms**
    
    When challenged that enforcement operates through "social mechanisms" or "appropriateness," Wilson responds that social norms fail to constitute enforcement.
    
    Without law backed by force, someone will eventually "dig up a dead body and do a dirty deed to it" simply because no physical force prevents the action.
    

- **The Secular Reliance on Force**:
    
    He concludes that secularists "are lying to us when they say that they want to rule because they're the only ones who can rule fairly; what they really want is a monopoly on force."
