    **Heuristic 1: The Barbell Strategy (Risk Containment)**

A dual strategy composed of extremes, “one safe and one speculative, deemed more robust than a ‘mono-modal’ strategy”. This is key to achieving antifragility by mitigating fragility.

- **Application (Finance/Life):** Put 85 to 90 percent “in extremely safe instruments” (the floor), and the remaining 10 to 15 percent in “extremely speculative bets, as leveraged as possible”. This provides a positive exposure to Black Swans without dependence on risk management errors.

- **Application (General):** “Clip your downside, protect yourself from extreme harm, and let the upside, the positive Black Swans, take care of itself”. We should “Provide for the worst; the best can take care of itself”.

**Heuristic 2: Via Negativa (Subtractive Knowledge)**

Focus on subtraction, or acts of omission, which are “more robust than those that add because addition may have unseen, complicated feedback loops”.

- **Rule of Thumb:** Charlatans give “positive advice, and only positive advice”. The professionals, selected by evolution, win by “not losing” or by focusing on “what to avoid”.

- **Application (Health):** “Removing medication, or some other unnatural stressor… is more robust than adding medication”. We should resort to medical techniques “only when the health payoff is very large (say, saving a life) and visibly exceeds its potential harm”.

- **Application (Knowledge/Prophecy):** Prophecy should be subtractive, “predicting the future by removing what is fragile from it rather than naively adding to it” (Subtractive Prophecy). We know what is wrong with “more certainty than we know anything else”.

**Heuristic 3: Skin in the Game (Symmetry and Responsibility)**

The central ethical rule is: “Thou shalt not have antifragility at the expense of the fragility of others”.

- **Core Rule:** Skin in the game means that you do not pay attention to what people say, “only to what they do, and to how much of their necks they are putting on the line”.

- **Application (Forecasting/Advice):** Predictions without skin in the game are dangerous. “Never ask the doctor what you should do. Ask him what he would do if he were in your place”. Fat Tony’s heuristic: “never get on a plane if the pilot is not on board”.

- **The Agency Problem:** Naive optimization is often driven by the “agency problem” where the manager benefits, but others (the principal or society) bear the fragility.

**Heuristic 4: Tinkering and Optionality**

Instead of top-down planning, embrace tinkering and trial and error, which harness “optionality” (the right to choose when to act).

- **Rule:** “Trial and error tinkering when it presents small errors and large gains”. The losses from errors must be limited and known, but the potential payoff is large (unbounded).

- **Optionality as Intelligence Substitute:** Having optionality means you “don’t have much need for what is commonly called intelligence, knowledge, insight, skills”. Optionality “is about getting the upper half of luck”.

- **The Rational Flâneur:** This approach is embodied by the “rational flâneur,” someone who “makes a decision opportunistically at every step to revise his schedule… so he can imbibe things based on new information obtained”.

**Heuristic 5: Less is More (Simplicity and Filtering)**

“Simpler methods for forecasting and inference can work much, much better than complicated ones”.

- **Filtering Information:** The best solution is “to only look at very large changes in data or conditions, never at small ones”. - **Decision Rule:** “If you have more than one reason to do something… just don’t do it”. “Obvious decisions (robust to error) require no more than a single reason”.

The goal is to establish a systematic protocol for determining when to intervene and when to leave systems alone. This approach emphasizes prudence, exposure detection, and respect for natural, bottom-up processes.

### A. The Foundational Shift: Exposure over Prediction

Naïve interventionism is inherently predictive (“Prediction as the child of modernity”). The antidote involves shifting focus entirely away from forecasting and toward detecting systemic exposure.

### 1. Fragility Detection

“It is far easier to figure out if something is fragile than to predict the occurrence of an event that may harm it”. This sensitivity to harm is “tractable, more so than forecasting the event that would cause the harm”.

### 2. Convexity Heuristic

Fragility resides in the nonlinear. We can detect the fragile by applying the “simple rule to detect the fragile”. If doubling the exposure more than doubles the harm, the system is fragile. The fragile is hurt “a lot more by extreme events than by a succession of intermediate ones”.

### 3. Thalesian Logic

Decision making must be based on payoffs and consequences, not intellectual certainty (Aristotelian logic). “Exposure is more important than knowledge; decision effects supersede logic”.When intervention is necessary, or when dealing with risk-taking activities, a specific asymmetric approach must be used.
