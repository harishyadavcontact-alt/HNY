# Beringia

> A landmass that emerges from the Bering Strait during ice ages, when sea levels drop. The bridge between the Old World and the New World — the gateway through which the ancestors of all indigenous Americans crossed into two vast, empty continents.

---

## Definition

**Beringia**: the landmass exposed between Siberia and Alaska when sea levels drop during ice ages, connecting Asia and North America across what is now the Bering Strait.

During the last ice age (~25,000–11,000 years ago), Beringia was a vast, partly inhabitable landscape — cold but not entirely frozen, with some vegetation and megafauna. Human populations lived there, possibly for generations, before or while crossing into North America.

---

## Why It Matters in the Evolutionary Framework

Beringia represents one of the most consequential [[Niche Switching|niche switches]] in human history. The humans who crossed it:
- Could not detect the New World from where they stood
- Were likely driven by pressure in the Old World (resource depletion, population pressure, conflict)
- Entered completely unknown territory — two vast continents of untouched ecosystems

> "The Beringians could not detect that the New World existed, but they could not stay in the Old World. They headed east into the unknown, into a forbidding landscape of rock and ice, of rough seas and dangerous terrain — and ultimately, into two vast continents of plenty."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## The Pattern

Beringia is an instance of the broader human pattern: entering unknown territory under pressure, with no certainty of what lies ahead. The same pattern appears in:
- Polynesians crossing the Pacific
- Early humans leaving Africa
- Modern exploration of space

Each is a niche switch: abandoning a saturated niche, accepting the risk of the unknown, occasionally discovering vastly greater opportunity.
