# Chesterton's Fence

> Do not remove a fence until you understand why it was built. Reforms should not be made to a system until the reasoning behind its current state is understood. Apparently irrational structures often encode forgotten wisdom.

---

## The Principle

G.K. Chesterton (1929): if you come across a fence in the middle of a road and cannot imagine why it was built, you should not tear it down. First discover why it was built. Then you will know whether it should be torn down.

The absence of an obvious reason for something is not evidence that there is no reason. Complex systems — whether ecological, social, cultural, or biological — often have structures that look arbitrary or irrational but encode hard-won adaptive information.

---

## In the Evolutionary Framework

Chesterton's Fence is a specific application of treating evolved systems with appropriate humility:

> "Removing functional organs because we can't tell what they're for is not wise."
> — Heying & Weinstein

Examples of Chesterton's Fences being violated:
- **Tonsillectomies** — once routine, later understood to be immunologically important
- **Appendix removal** — once considered vestigial, now understood to house gut microbiome
- **Food taboos** — dismissed as superstition, sometimes encoding real pathogen-avoidance wisdom
- **Cultural rituals** — stripped away as irrational, sometimes encoding important social functions

---

## Cultural Application

Cultural practices are evolutionary Chesterton's Fences. They persist because they worked — even if the adaptive reason is not visible to the present generation ([[Literally False, Metaphorically True]]). Before dismantling a cultural practice, first ask why it exists.

This does not mean cultural practices should never change. It means the burden of proof is on the reformer: demonstrate that you understand why the fence was built before you tear it down.
