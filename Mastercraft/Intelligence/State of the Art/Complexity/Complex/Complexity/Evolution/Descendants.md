# Descendants

> The organisms that carry a lineage forward. A lineage is defined as an individual and all of its descendants. Descendants are what evolution is for — getting genes into the future through them.

---

## In the Context of Lineage

A lineage is precisely defined: an individual and **all of that individual's descendants**. This is what gives the concept of lineage its fractal power — every level of descent, from child to civilization, is a lineage.

Descendants are not merely offspring. They include:
- Direct offspring (children)
- Offspring's offspring (grandchildren, great-grandchildren)
- All future generations derived from a given ancestor

This is what selection is "trying" to maximize — not just your immediate reproductive output, but the persistence of your lineage's genes through all your descendants, indefinitely far into the future.

---

## Why Descendants Matter More Than Offspring

The tautological definition of fitness: **lodging your genes as far into the future as possible**. This means descendants across many generations matter — not just the first generation.

An organism that has many offspring but whose descendants die out after two generations has achieved less fitness than one with fewer offspring whose lineage persists for thousands of years.

This is why eusocial workers who don't reproduce individually can still be evolutionarily successful — they invest in descendants through the reproductive members of their colony.
