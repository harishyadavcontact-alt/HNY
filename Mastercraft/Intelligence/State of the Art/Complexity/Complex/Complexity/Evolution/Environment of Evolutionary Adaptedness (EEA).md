# Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness (EEA)

> The environment that favored the evolution of a given adaptive trait. Humans have many EEAs, not just that of the African savannahs. Every adaptation has its own EEA — the set of conditions under which it was selected for. When those conditions no longer exist, the adaptation may become a mismatch.

---

## Definition

**EEA (Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness)**: the historical environment in which a particular trait evolved — the conditions under which natural selection shaped that trait into its current form.

Not a single place or time: humans have many EEAs, one for each trait. The EEA for your immune system is different from the EEA for your social instincts, which is different from the EEA for your food preferences.

---

## Why It Matters

Understanding the EEA of a trait tells you:
1. **What problem it was solving** — what selection pressures shaped it
2. **What environment it "expects"** — the conditions it was calibrated for
3. **Where it might misfire** — when modern conditions differ from the EEA, the trait may produce maladaptive results

---

## The Mismatch Problem

When the current environment differs substantially from the EEA, we get **evolutionary mismatch** — adaptations that worked perfectly in their EEA producing poor or harmful outcomes in the present.

Examples:
- **Sugar and fat cravings** → EEA: caloric scarcity; Modern: caloric abundance → obesity, metabolic disease
- **Status competition** → EEA: small groups of known individuals; Modern: anonymous mass society → dysfunctional status signaling
- **In-group favoritism** → EEA: tribal survival; Modern: global interdependence → tribalism and conflict

This is the core of the [[Hyper-Novelty]] problem: we are organisms with many EEAs that no longer exist.

---

## Humans Have Many EEAs

There is no single ancestral human environment. Our lineage has inhabited:
- African savannahs and coasts
- Forests, grasslands, arctic tundra
- Agricultural settlements
- Pre-modern cities

Each environment shaped different adaptations over different timescales. This is why humans are extreme generalists — we have accumulated adaptations from many EEAs.
