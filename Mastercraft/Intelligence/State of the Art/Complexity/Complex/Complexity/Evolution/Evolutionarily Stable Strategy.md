# Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)

> A tactic that, once adopted by most members of a population, is invulnerable to displacement by competing strategies. An ESS cannot be "invaded" — any mutant using a different strategy will do worse. The ESS is the endpoint of evolution within a game-theoretic framework.

---

## Definition

An **Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)** is a strategy such that, if most members of a population adopt it, no alternative strategy can invade and spread through natural selection.

First formalized by John Maynard Smith and George Price (1973). The ESS concept imports game theory into evolutionary biology.

---

## Why It Matters

Evolution doesn't just select for the best strategy in isolation — it selects for the best strategy *given what others are doing*. The ESS is the strategy that is stable against all alternatives in the context of a given population.

A strategy is an ESS if:
1. It performs as well as any alternative when competing against itself (no invasion by identical strategy)
2. It outperforms any rare mutant strategy that tries to invade

---

## Classic Example: Hawk-Dove

- **Hawks** always fight for resources
- **Doves** always retreat when challenged

A population of all-Hawks: constant costly fights → select for some Doves
A population of all-Doves: free ride for the first Hawk → select for more Hawks
The ESS: a mixed frequency of Hawks and Doves (or individuals who play mixed strategies)

---

## Application to Civilization

Heying and Weinstein invoke ESS as a design criterion for the [[The Fourth Frontier|fourth frontier]]:

> "We need a system that is an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy, a strategy incapable of invasion by competitors that would betray its core values."
> — Heying & Weinstein

A civilization designed as an ESS:
- Cannot be taken over by narrow interests
- Is robust to exploitation and defection
- Maintains its core values even as the game evolves around it
