# History

> History is what happened. In evolutionary biology, understanding history — who is related to whom, what changed when, what environments ancestors inhabited — is the foundation of all predictions about present organisms. We are the descendants of history.

---

## History in Evolutionary Biology

Evolutionary biology is fundamentally a **historical science**. Every organism alive today is the product of an unbroken chain of ancestors stretching back billions of years. To understand why an organism does what it does, you often need to understand what its ancestors were doing and in what environment.

Key historical inquiries in evolutionary biology:
- **Phylogenetics**: who is related to whom, and when did lineages diverge?
- **Paleoecology**: what environments did ancestral populations inhabit?
- **EEA (Environment of Evolutionary Adaptedness)**: what conditions shaped the adaptations we carry today?

---

## History as the Arbiter of Adaptation

An adaptation makes sense only in the context of the history that produced it. The human propensity to crave sugar and fat is not a mistake — it is a historically adaptive response to scarcity that is now maladaptive in abundance.

Understanding history is what allows us to distinguish:
- Ancestrally-adapted traits that still serve their original function
- Ancestrally-adapted traits that are now mismatched with the present environment ([[Hyper-Novelty]])
- Novel traits that have emerged since the ancestral environment

---

## History and Hyper-Novelty

The central problem of the 21st century, in the Heying-Weinstein framework, is that we are organisms built for a history that no longer exists. We carry adaptations shaped by millions of years of ancestral experience — but we now live in an environment that differs radically from that experience.

History is why we are who we are. Understanding it is what allows us to navigate being who we are in a world we weren't built for.
