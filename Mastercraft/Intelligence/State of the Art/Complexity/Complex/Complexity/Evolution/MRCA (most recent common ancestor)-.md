# MRCA — Most Recent Common Ancestor

> That ancestral organism through whom two clades are most closely related. The foundational concept for reconstructing evolutionary history: to understand how two lineages are related, find their most recent common ancestor.

---

## Definition

The **Most Recent Common Ancestor (MRCA)** of two or more organisms is the most recent organism from which all of them are directly descended.

- The MRCA of you and your sibling is one of your parents
- The MRCA of you and your cousin is one of your grandparents
- The MRCA of humans and chimpanzees is an ancestral ape that lived roughly 6-7 million years ago
- The MRCA of birds and crocodiles is an archosaur that lived ~250 million years ago

---

## Why It Matters

The MRCA concept is foundational to [[Historical Science|macroevolutionary biology]]. The goal of reconstructing evolutionary relationships is, in essence, finding MRCAs. If you know the MRCA of two groups, you know when their lineages diverged and what shared characters they inherited from that ancestor.

Confusing shared ancestral characters with shared derived characters is one of the classic errors in phylogenetics: two species may share a feature not because they are closely related, but because they both inherited it from a distant common ancestor.

---

## Lineage and MRCA

In Weinstein's definition of [[Lineage]]:

> "A lineage is an individual and all of that individual's descendants."

The MRCA defines the starting point of any lineage you care to examine. The MRCA of this room's occupants is some shared ancestor — and that individual plus all descendants constitutes the lineage that "this room" represents.
