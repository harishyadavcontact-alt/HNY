# Sucker's Folly

> The tendency of a concentrated short-term benefit not only to obscure risk and long-term cost, but also to drive acceptance even when the net analysis is negative. The fundamental mechanism of civilizational self-destruction.

---

## Definition

**Sucker's Folly**: the cognitive trap in which a short-term benefit is so salient and immediate that it drowns out awareness of the long-term cost — even when explicit analysis would reveal the cost outweighs the benefit.

The structure of the trap:
1. Short-term gain is visible, tangible, immediate
2. Long-term cost is diffuse, abstract, delayed
3. The short-term gain dominates decision-making even when the net analysis is negative

---

## Why It Exists

Sucker's Folly is an evolutionary product. In the ancestral environment, immediate tangible resources were real. Diffuse long-term risks were harder to perceive and act upon. Selection favored sensitivity to immediate gains.

In a hyper-novel world with technology that amplifies both benefits and costs to civilizational scales, this ancestral bias becomes dangerous.

---

## Civilizational Examples

- **Cheap industrial food**: low-cost calories obscuring metabolic, ecological, and social costs
- **Growth economics**: the benefit of growth is immediate and measurable; the cost of unsustainability is diffuse and future
- **Social media**: immediate engagement rewards obscuring long-term attention and social cohesion costs
- **Dimensional lumber and carpentered corners**: a productivity win that, we later learned, literally changed how humans perceive space

> "Our economic and political system, in combination with our desire for growth in the moment, inflicts policies and behaviors that don't seem crazy at first — and yet they often turn out to be not only bad for us and the planet, but also irreversible."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## The Corrective

Awareness of Sucker's Folly is necessary but not sufficient to escape it. The corrective involves:
- Deliberately extending the time horizon of analysis
- Assigning weight to diffuse and future costs
- Building systems that make long-term costs visible (externality pricing, precautionary regulation)
- Chesterton's Fence thinking: ask why things are the way they are before changing them
