# Survival

> The first prerequisite for fitness. An organism that doesn't survive cannot reproduce. But survival is not the goal — it is the precondition. The goal is getting genes to the future. Survival is one strategy; sometimes other strategies outcompete it.

---

## Survival and Fitness

"Survival of the fittest" is the common shorthand for natural selection. But this is almost a tautology (what's fittest? the stuff that survives), and it also misleads by implying survival is the primary criterion.

Survival is necessary but not sufficient. An organism that survives but does not reproduce contributes nothing to the future. An organism that dies reproducing has contributed everything it could.

The real criterion is **lodging your genes as far into the future as possible** — which usually requires survival, but not always.

---

## When Survival is Sacrificed

- **Semelparous salmon**: reproduce once in a massive burst, then die. Fitness maximized by spending everything on one reproductive event.
- **Eusocial workers**: worker ants, bees, wasps sacrifice themselves for the colony. Their genes persist through the queen they serve.
- **Soldiers**: die protecting the group whose survival perpetuates the lineage's genes.

In each case, individual survival is traded for lineage-level fitness. The genes are indifferent to whether the individual survives — they care only about whether they reach the future.

---

## Survival and Hyper-Novelty

In the ancestral environment, survival instincts and fitness were well-aligned. In the modern world ([[Hyper-Novelty]]), they can diverge dangerously. Behaviors that felt survival-oriented (consuming sugar, fat, status signaling, tribalism) were adaptive in scarcity but destructive in abundance.

We are exquisitely designed for survival in environments we no longer live in.
