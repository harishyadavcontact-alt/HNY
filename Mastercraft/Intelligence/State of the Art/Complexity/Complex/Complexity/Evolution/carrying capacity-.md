# Carrying Capacity

> The maximum number of individuals that can be stably supported, at equilibrium, by a given spatiotemporal opportunity. When a population hits its carrying capacity, growth stops and competition intensifies. Every population is always approaching, oscillating around, or retreating from its carrying capacity.

---

## Definition

**Carrying capacity** (K): the maximum population size that an environment can sustainably support given available resources. At carrying capacity, births equal deaths and population size stabilizes.

Example: X number of wolves in Yellowstone in 1900 — the number sustainable given the prey base, territory, and other constraints of that specific time and place.

---

## Population Dynamics

Populations oscillate around carrying capacity:
- **Below K**: resources are abundant, reproduction exceeds mortality, population grows
- **At K**: equilibrium — birth rate equals death rate
- **Above K**: resources are depleted, mortality exceeds reproduction, population declines

Growth feels good — it is what "winning" feels like in evolutionary terms. Equilibrium at K feels like constraint. This is why organisms are cognitively biased toward growth ([[The Fourth Frontier]]).

---

## Carrying Capacity and Human History

Humans repeatedly exceeded local carrying capacities through:
1. **Geographic expansion** (Beringia, oceanic crossings)
2. **Technological innovation** (agriculture, industry)
3. **Resource transfer** (conquest, trade)

Each expansion temporarily raised the effective carrying capacity. But no expansion raises it permanently — every population eventually reaches a new equilibrium.

> "Creatures seek untapped opportunity, and they exploit it. Successful exploitation of novel opportunities temporarily raises the limit on the number of individuals that can live in a given habitat."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## The Modern Problem

We have reached global carrying capacity constraints. Geographic frontiers are exhausted. Technological expansion has environmental limits. The "growth obsession" that was adaptive when frontiers existed is now dangerous when the planet is a closed system.
