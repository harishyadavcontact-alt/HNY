# Eusociality

> A social system in which some individuals forgo reproduction in order to facilitate the reproduction of others. Eusocial populations function as superorganisms. This is where individual reproductive success as a proxy for fitness breaks down — and lineage selection takes over.

---

## Definition

Eusociality: some members of the colony forego personal reproduction entirely to facilitate the reproduction of other colony members (typically the queen or dominant reproducer).

Examples:
- **Ants, bees, wasps** (Hymenoptera) — workers are all female, do not reproduce, facilitate the queen
- **Termites** — workers do not reproduce, facilitate the queen
- **Naked mole rats** — non-breeding workers serve the breeding female

---

## The Paradox That Troubled Darwin

Darwin spent a great deal of time worrying about ants because they seem to violate natural selection:
- Worker ants cooperate maximally
- Worker ants sacrifice reproduction completely
- How can selection produce organisms that don't reproduce?

The standard resolution: **haplodiploidy** in Hymenoptera (ants, bees, wasps). Due to their unusual genetic system, female workers are more closely related to their sisters (0.75) than to their own offspring (0.5). So they propagate more of their genes by raising sisters than by reproducing personally.

---

## The Bigger Problem: Termites and Naked Mole Rats

> "We rarely teach that termites do the exact same thing. They're eusocial. The workers don't reproduce. They facilitate the reproduction of the queen. And they don't have the weird genetic system."
> — Bret Weinstein, Sophia Lecture 2

Termites: eusocial, no haplodiploidy.
Naked mole rats: eusocial, no haplodiploidy.

This means the haplodiploidy explanation was a satisfying partial answer that made us stop looking. The real answer is **[[Lineage]] selection**: workers are not reproductive failures — they are optimizing lineage fitness at a higher level than individual reproduction.

---

## The Implication

> "There's an argument to be made that humans are also eusocial — a very special case."
> — Bret Weinstein

Celibate clergy who spend their lives building cathedrals. Soldiers who die without offspring. Teachers and monks and scientists who contribute to the lineage without personal reproduction.

At the lineage level, these are not evolutionary failures. The question is always: *which level of lineage is being served?*
