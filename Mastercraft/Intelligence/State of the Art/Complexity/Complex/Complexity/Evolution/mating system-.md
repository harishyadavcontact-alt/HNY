# Mating System

> The pattern of matings between individuals of a population — specifically, the typical number of simultaneous mates each sex has. Mating systems are shaped by the distribution of resources, parental investment asymmetries, and the operational sex ratio.

---

## Definition

**Mating system**: the characteristic pattern of who mates with whom in a population, including:
- The number of simultaneous mates each individual has
- Whether pair bonds persist after mating
- How much each sex invests in offspring

---

## Major Types

| System | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Monogamy** | One male, one female | Most songbirds, many human societies |
| **Polygyny** | One male, multiple females | Gorillas, elephant seals, some human societies |
| **Polyandry** | One female, multiple males | Jacanas, some human societies |
| **Promiscuity** | No lasting pair bonds | Chimpanzees, many insects |

---

## What Shapes Mating Systems

Mating systems are adaptive — they evolve based on:
1. **Parental investment**: the sex that invests more in offspring is more selective
2. **Resource distribution**: how easy it is for one sex to monopolize resources or mates
3. **Operational sex ratio**: the ratio of sexually active males to females at any given time
4. **Offspring viability**: whether two parents are needed for offspring to survive

---

## Human Mating Systems

Humans show evidence of mixed mating system history:
- Pair-bonding, biparental care, concealed ovulation → consistent with monogamy
- Male-male competition, sexual size dimorphism → consistent with polygyny history
- Cross-cultural variation → no single system dominates

This is a feature of a highly generalist, highly variable species ([[generalist-]]).
