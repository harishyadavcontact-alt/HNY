# Phenotype

> The observable structural and functional properties of an individual — what the genotype actually produces. Bodies, behaviors, and physiological traits are all phenotypic. Selection acts on phenotypes.

---

## Definition

**Phenotype**: the observable characteristics of an organism — everything that results from the interaction of the genotype with developmental processes and the environment.

Phenotypic characters include:
- **Morphological**: body shape, size, limb structure, tooth form
- **Physiological**: metabolic rate, immune function, brain organization
- **Behavioral**: migration patterns, mating displays, social behaviors
- **Developmental**: the sequence and timing of developmental events

---

## Why It Matters

Selection acts on phenotypes: organisms that survive and reproduce do so because of what they express, not just what they carry in their DNA. A gene that produces a beneficial phenotype in one environment may produce a harmful phenotype in another.

This is why the relationship between genotype and phenotype is not simple 1:1 mapping:
- Same genotype → different phenotypes in different environments (phenotypic plasticity)
- Different genotypes → same phenotype (convergent evolution — same solution reached independently)
- Same genotype, different cell types → different gene expression (most of your genome is silent in any given cell)

---

## Phenotype and Evolution

> "Selection acts on phenotypes. It sorts genotypes."

You cannot select for a gene you cannot see. Genes persist into the future because the phenotypes they produce (or enable, or don't prevent) are selected for. This is why [[Genes — Unbridled Ambition Zero Vanity|genes with zero vanity]] — genes that produce no phenotypic effect — can still persist: they just don't get selected against.
