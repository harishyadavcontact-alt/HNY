# Theory of Mind

> The ability to infer mental states — beliefs, emotions, knowledge — of others, especially when they differ from one’s own. The capacity to understand that another mind sees a different world than yours. Staggeringly useful. Not uniquely human.

---

## The Definition

Theory of mind: the ability to model what another individual knows, believes, or feels — and to recognize that their mental state may differ from your own.

To have theory of mind is to understand that *your* knowledge is not universal. Other minds contain different information, formed from different vantage points, with different implications for behavior.

> "These baboons have theory of mind. They have the ability to discern that what they think is true about something is not necessarily what some other baboon thinks is true about something."
> — Heather Heying, Sophia Lecture 4

---

## Why It Is Staggeringly Useful

Social life is fundamentally a problem of inference: what does this other individual know, want, intend? Without theory of mind, social interaction is a crude matching of behavioral signals. With it, you can:
- Predict what others will do based on what they know (or don’t know)
- Deceive — presenting false information to manipulate another’s model
- Cooperate efficiently — coordinating on shared knowledge
- Teach — understanding what the learner doesn’t yet know

Theory of mind is the engine of complex social life.

---

## It Is Not Uniquely Human

Theory of mind appears in:
- **Baboons** — recognize that other baboons hold different knowledge, react with surprise when social rules are violated (demonstrating expectations based on models of others’ knowledge)
- **Elephants, toothed whales, crows, primates** — all show evidence of modeling others’ mental states
- **Great apes** — extensive experimental evidence

> "We see the rudiments of this capacity in many other species, and we see it extensively elaborated in a highly cooperative few, such as elephants, toothed whales (such as dolphins), crows, and many nonhuman primates."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## The Human Version

Humans are by far the most elaborated theory of mind in the history of life. We can:
- Model the mental states of people we have never met
- Project into the past and future minds of others
- Use language to pass a complex abstraction from one mind to another with remarkable precision

> "We can accurately pass a complex abstraction from one mind to another by simply vibrating the air between us. It is everyday magic that usually passes without our notice."
> — Heying & Weinstein

---

## How Theory of Mind Works

To model another’s mind, you run a kind of emulation — a simulation of the other person within your own cognitive architecture. This requires you to hold two simultaneous models: what *I* know, and what *you* know. To compare them, both must be rendered in the same currency — subjective experience.

This is why theory of mind is deeply linked to consciousness: the more elaborate the theory of mind, the more sophisticated the underlying self-model that enables it.

---

## Shared Consciousness

Theory of mind scaled up becomes shared consciousness: a collective cognitive space between individuals where ideas are held in common and co-developed. No individual owns the shared space, but all participants contribute to it and draw from it.

> "Shared consciousness is an emergent, intangible space between people, where concepts are lodged and co-cultivated. Each participant has a distinct perspective on the space, much as each witness to a physical event will have a somewhat different vantage point, but the space is a property of the collective."
> — Heying & Weinstein

The campfire, the seminar, the civilization — these are all instantiations of shared consciousness built on the foundation of theory of mind.

---

## Collective vs Individual Consciousness

Individual consciousness likely did not evolve first. It evolved *in parallel* with collective consciousness — the ability to model others’ minds, and to pool cognitive resources in a shared space.

The individual mind is partly constituted by its embeddedness in shared consciousness. To be a full human mind is to be woven into a network of other minds.
