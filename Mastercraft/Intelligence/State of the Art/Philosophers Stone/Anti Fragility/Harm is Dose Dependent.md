> [!important]
> 
> Our antifragility have conditions. The frequency of stressors matters a bit.
> 
> - Acute Stressors: win acute stress with ample time to rest
>     
>     Humans tend to do better with acute than with chronic stressors, **particularly when the former are followed by ample time for recovery, which allows the stressors to do their jobs as messengers.**
>     
> 
> - high level stress with recovery is helpful
>     
>     For instance, having an intense emotional shock from seeing a snake coming out of my keyboard or a vampire entering my room, followed by a period of soothing safety (with chamomile tea and baroque music) long enough for me to regain control of my emotions, would be beneficial for my health, provided of course that I manage to overcome the snake or vampire after an arduous, hopefully heroic fight and have a picture taken next to the dead predator.
>     
> 
> - chronic stressors: mild but continued stress
>     
>     Such a stressor would be certainly better than the mild but continuous stress of a boss, mortgage, tax problems, guilt over procrastinating with one’s tax return, exam pressures, chores, emails to answer, forms to complete, daily commutes—things that make you feel trapped in life.
>     
> 
> - low level stress without recovery is harmfull
>     
>     In other words, the pressures brought about by civilization. In fact, neurobiologists show that the former type of stressor is necessary, the second harmful, for one’s health.
>     
>     For an idea of how harmful a low-level stressor without recovery can be, consider the so-called Chinese water torture: a drop continuously hitting the same spot on your head, never letting you recover.
>     
> 
> - disruption of recovery = end of antifragility
>     
>     Indeed, the way Heracles managed to control Hydra was by cauterizing the wounds on the stumps of the heads that he had just severed. He thus prevented the regrowth of the heads and the exercise of antifragility. In other words, he disrupted the recovery.
>
