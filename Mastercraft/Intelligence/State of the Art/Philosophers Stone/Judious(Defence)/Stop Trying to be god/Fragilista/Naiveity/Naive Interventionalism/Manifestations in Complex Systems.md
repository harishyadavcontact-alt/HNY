- 1. The Opacity Problem
    
    Outside of physics, and generally in complex domains, the reasons behind things have had a tendency to make themselves less obvious to us, and even less to the fragilista. This property of natural things not to advertise themselves in a user’s manual is, alas, not much of a hindrance: some fragilistas will get together to write the user’s manual themselves, thanks to their definition of “science.”
    

- 2. The Linear Illusion (Procrustean Bed)
    
    Interventionists treat organisms and complex systems like simple machines. Where simplifications fail and cause the most damage is when “something nonlinear is simplified with the linear as a substitute”. They “blow them up with our fear of randomness and love of smoothness”.
    

- 3. The Multiplicative Effect (Opacity)
    
    Complications resulting from interventions lead to “multiplicative chains of unanticipated effects”. Due to opacity, an intervention leads to unforeseen consequences, prompting another intervention to correct the secondary effects, resulting in “an explosive series of branching ‘unforeseen’ responses, each one worse than the preceding one”.
    

**Naïve rationalism** is a foundational intellectual error that underlies naïve interventionism and the denial of complexity and fragility. It is the belief that formal knowledge and explicit reasoning are sufficient to understand and control complex systems, leading to a profound overestimation of human understanding.

**Complexification and Compounding Errors:** The naïve rationalist often introduces complexity, believing it is sophisticated. However, in complex systems, complications lead to “multiplicative chains of unanticipated effects”. The urge to intervene leads to unforeseen consequences, followed by further interventions to correct the secondary effects, leading to an “explosive series of branching ‘unforeseen’ responses, each one worse than the preceding one”.

### 4. The Mediocristan to Extremistan Transition

Beyond municipal noise, the same logic applies to: the child who, after spending time in a sterilized environment, is left out in the open; a system with dictated political stability from the top; the effects of price controls; the advantages of size for a corporation; etc. We switch from a system that produces steady but controllable volatility (Mediocristan), closer to the statistical “bell curve” (from the benign family of the Gaussian or Normal Distribution), into one that is highly unpredictable and moves mostly by jumps, called “fat tails.” Fat tails—a synonym for Extremistan—mean that remote events, those in what is called the “tails,” play a disproportionate role. One (first graph) is volatile; it fluctuates but does not sink. The other (second graph) sinks without significant fluctuations outside of episodes of turmoil. In the long run the second system will be far more volatile—but volatility comes in lumps. When we constrain the first system we tend to get the second outcome.

### 5. Medical Examples

In medicine, we tend to “over-intervene in areas with minimal benefits (and large risks) while under-intervening in areas in which intervention is necessary, like emergencies”. Examples of naïve intervention leading to iatrogenics include unnecessary tonsillectomies and the use of Thalidomide, where “the benefits appeared to be obvious and immediate, though small, and the harm remained delayed for years”.

### 6. Socioeconomic and Political Examples

Social scientists and economists lack “built-in consciousness of iatrogenics”. Socioeconomic life and the human body are areas “in which we have historically combined a low degree of competence with a high rate of intervention”. In political systems, overstabilization makes systems vulnerable to Black Swans. Centralized planning, as seen in the Chinese famine of 1959–1961, can amplify problems due to “inflexibility in the procurement system”.

### 7. Information Overload

The inability to distinguish “noise from signal is behind overintervention”. The obsession with data leads to iatrogenics: “the more data you get, the less you know what’s going on, and the more iatrogenics you will cause”.
