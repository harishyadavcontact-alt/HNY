1. Naive Rationalism
    
    1. Naive rationalism is characterized by an overreliance on theory, planning, and codified knowledge while mistaking the unknown for the nonexistent.
    
    1. Naive rationalism, or the “Soviet-Harvard delusion,” is the (unscientific) “overestimation of the reach of scientific knowledge”.
    
    1. Thinking that the reasons for things are, by default, accessible to university buildings. Also called the Soviet-Harvard illusion.
    

  

1. • The Belief in Accessible Reasons
    
    1. : A naive rationalist is someone who “believes that the reasons behind things are automatically accessible to him”.  
        
    

1. • The Fragilista
    
    1. : The naive rationalist is often referred to as a “fragilista”. The fragilista “defaults to thinking that what he doesn’t see is not there, or what he does not understand does not exist”. At the core, he tends to “mistake the unknown for the nonexistent”.  
        
    

1. • The Error of Conflation:
    
    1. Naive rationalism often conflates “rationalizing with rational—the two are almost always exact opposites”.
    

1. • The Danger of Formalism:
    
    1. The core error is the application of “Platonic forms” and formalized models to messy, real-world complexity, leading to “Platonicity,” which is the love of “some crisp abstract forms” that make us “blind to the mess of reality”. These models are “like potentially helpful medicines that carry random but very severe side effects”.
    

1. The Turkey Problem in
    
    1. : The “mother of all harmful mistakes” is “mistaking absence of evidence (of harm) for evidence of absence”.
    
    1. The list of drugs, such as Thalidomide and Diethylstilbestrol, where benefits were small and immediate but harm was large and delayed, exemplifies this failure of naive rationalism
