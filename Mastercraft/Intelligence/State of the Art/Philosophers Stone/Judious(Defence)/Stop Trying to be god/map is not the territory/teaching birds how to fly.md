Ohhh yes—**"teaching birds how to fly"** is another classic **Taleb-ism** (and adjacent to the Soviet–Harvard delusion). It's a savage metaphor that exposes **the arrogance of taking credit for things that happen naturally**.

---

### 🐦 **What does “teaching birds how to fly” mean?**

It’s the **illusion that something happened** _**because**_ **of our intervention**, when in reality it would’ve happened anyway—**naturally, organically, bottom-up**.

> It’s confusing correlation with causation.

---

### 🔍 Breakdown:

- Birds fly **not because humans taught them**, but because it’s in their nature.

- But the delusional intellectual thinks,
    
    > “Look! I held a bird in the air and flapped its wings—it flew. I must have taught it.”
    

- In reality, the bird was always going to fly. Your "training" was noise.

---

### 🎯 Applied Meaning (Taleb-style):

1. **Education**
    
    - Kids learn to speak just by being around language.
    
    - But schools take credit for “teaching them” when most of the learning is _absorbed naturally_.
    

1. **Economics**
    
    - Entrepreneurs build and adapt from trial and error.
    
    - Academics write papers after the fact and claim they created the system.
    

1. **Startups & Policy**
    
    - A founder builds something great.
    
    - An investor or policymaker shows up and says, “We enabled that success.”
    
    - No you didn’t—the system was already flying.
    

---

### 🔄 Related Concepts:

- **Post hoc fallacy**: Assuming that because B happened after A, A caused B.

- **Narrative fallacy**: Creating a neat story where causality is vague.

- **Overfitting**: Applying theories where nature already has better heuristics.

---

### ⚔️ Taleb’s Burn:

> “Academics are often just birds taking credit for the wind beneath their wings.”

---

### 💥 TL;DR:

> “Teaching birds to fly” = taking credit for natural processes, or mistaking your theory for the source of real-world success.
