- **First Principles:** These are foundational assumptions that cannot be deduced from any other assumption. Thinking from first principles allows you to deduce truths logically rather than relying on the consensus of authorities.
- **The Precautionary Principle & Chesterton’s Fence:** Both concepts urge caution when tinkering with ancient or complex systems. Chesterton’s Fence states that you should never remove a barrier (or a tradition/biological structure) until you understand why it was put there in the first place. The Precautionary Principle advises that when the negative outcomes of a system are highly uncertain, changes should be made slowly, if at all ("just because you can, doesn't mean you should").
- **Trade-offs:** The fundamental biological and economic rule that you cannot maximize every trait or value at the same time. For example, _allocation trade-offs_ dictate that using finite resources for one thing means taking them from another (e.g., growing larger antlers costs bone density elsewhere). _Design constraint trade-offs_ dictate that you cannot physically be both the fastest and the most maneuverable.
- **The Sucker’s Folly:** The dangerous tendency for a concentrated, short-term benefit to blind us to long-term risks and costs. This drives people to accept systems or behaviors that are ultimately terrible for them in the long run.
- **Hyper-novelty:** The condition of the modern world where the rate of technological and social change is so rapid that our brains, bodies, and social systems are perpetually out of sync with our environment.
- **Paradoxes and Productive Tautologies:** In science, a paradox (two things that seem true but contradict each other) shouldn't be a source of frustration, but rather an "X on a treasure map" that tells you to "dig here" because there is an important discovery to be made. Conversely, a "productive tautology" is a statement that is so obvious it cannot help but be true, but stating it clearly helps us analyze things we don't yet understand.
- **Scientism vs. Science:** "Scientism" is a fake version of science where institutions merely imitate the _language_ and _methods_ of science (like assigning numbers to things that can't easily be quantified) without actually doing rigorous scientific analysis.
- **Reductionism:** The frequent mistake of taking a highly complex system and reducing it to just a few easily measurable parts. For instance, treating the human body strictly like a "finely calibrated machine" to be manipulated with pharmaceutical switches often creates new problems and masks the original ones.
- **The Naturalistic Fallacy:** The mistaken assumption that whatever "is" in nature is exactly what "ought to be". Recognizing that a terrible behavior (like war or starvation) has evolutionary roots does _not_ mean we should accept it as morally good.
