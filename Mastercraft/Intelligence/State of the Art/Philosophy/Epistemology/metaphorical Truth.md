Metaphorical truth is a concept describing beliefs or claims that may not be literally or factually true, but behaving as if they were true produces better outcomes than behaving according to their literal falsehood.

## Definition and Core Framework

- Things that you do better if you act as if they are true than if you act according to the fact that they are false". These are beliefs that cannot help but be true in a practical sense, even if they fail tests of literal accuracy.

## Key Characteristics

**Adaptive Value**: Metaphorical truths function as guides that enhance survival, cooperation, and flourishing. From an evolutionary perspective, they represent cultural wisdom accumulated over time through trial and error.

**Pragmatic Over Literal**: Unlike correspondence theories of truth (which evaluate whether statements match objective reality), metaphorical truth emphasizes the functional consequences of belief. The question shifts from "Is this factually accurate?" to "Does believing this lead to better outcomes?".

**Religious and Ethical Applications**: Many religious traditions contain metaphorical truths. For example, even if there's "no actual being in the sky looking down on us, it doesn't mean you're not way better off thinking as if there is". The behavioral and psychological benefits—moral guidance, community cohesion, meaning-making—justify treating these beliefs as operationally true.

## Evolutionary Perspective

Metaphor is essential in science as well, not just religion.

Scientists use metaphors to bridge gaps in knowledge, creating conceptual frameworks that allow them to **"skip from this zone that we understand to that zone that we understand".**

As scientific understanding advances, some metaphors get replaced by more precise models, but metaphorical thinking remains foundational to inquiry.

Human traditions often contain adaptive wisdom whose mechanisms we don't fully understand. Discarding beliefs simply because they're not literally true risks losing their beneficial effects—what Weinstein calls hitting "load-bearing pillars" in cultural architecture.
