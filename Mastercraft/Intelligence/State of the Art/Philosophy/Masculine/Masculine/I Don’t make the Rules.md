# Conclusion: Four Paths from a Single Problem

Each of these philosophers confronts the same fundamental problem—the persistence of human violence—but they arrive at vastly different conclusions about its nature, its source, and its solution. The following table provides a summary of their core positions.

|**Concept**|**John Locke**|**Carl Schmitt**|**Leo Strauss**|**René Girard**|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**View of Human Nature**|An unknowable "X" whose true substance cannot be known, only its external powers and preferences.|Defined by an inescapable condition of being divided by fundamental questions and forced to take sides.|A knowable but deeply problematic truth that is too dangerous to be spoken of openly.|Defined by an unparalleled aptitude for mimesis (imitation), which extends to desire and leads to rivalry.|
|**Root of Violence**|Debates over unanswerable questions of religion and virtue that interfere with peaceful commerce.|The permanent political reality of division, which forces humans to distinguish between friends and enemies.|The violent, criminal origins of all political orders, such as the murder of Remus by Romulus.|Escalating mimetic rivalry that culminates in the "founding murder" of a unanimous scapegoat.|
|**Proposed Solution**|Make dangerous questions private and redirect human energy toward the infinite accumulation of wealth.|Acknowledge the enemy and embrace the political struggle, even to the point of a new crusade, to rediscover meaning.|Acknowledge dangerous truths esoterically while leaving the "sad exigencies" of founding violence veiled.|No explicit solution is offered, only a final choice between limitless violence and peace.|
|**Modern Blind Spot**|Forgetting about the parts of the world that never agreed to make questions of religion private.|The danger that in fighting one's enemy, one will become just like them, leading to a Pyrrhic victory.|The paralyzing self-knowledge that awareness of the city's problematic nature makes an unreflective, patriotic defense of it impossible.|The shared denial of liberals and conservatives to acknowledge mimetic violence, leading them to systematically underestimate the risk of apocalyptic conflict.|
