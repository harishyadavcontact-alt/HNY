The Capitalist Peace

- Privatize the most dangerous questions. He proposed a world where matters of ultimate virtue, the good life, and true religion were removed from the public sphere and made into matters of personal, private conviction. This was a form of strategic retreat.

- The modern world would agree to stop fighting over these questions because the only way to stop people from killing each other was to ensure that "nobody thought about it too much."

- For Locke and the architects of the Enlightenment, the intellectual cost of not debating these ultimate truths seemed a "small price to pay" for peace.

- To justify this retreat, Locke proposed **a new understanding of human nature—or rather, a new way of not understanding i**t.

- He argued that the true, essential substance of a person is an unknowable "X". We can observe a person's external behaviors, preferences, and "powers," but we can never truly know their inner essence.

- This very unknowability, paradoxically, becomes the foundation for classic liberal rights:
    
    - **Freedom of Religion**: We cannot know what people are truly thinking in the "temple of their minds."
    
    - **Freedom of Speech**: We cannot irrefutably criticize how people express their inner selves.
    
    - **Right to Property**: We cannot second-guess what people choose to do with their possessions.
    

- Because no one is in an authoritative position to challenge the inner desires of this unknowable "X," society must leave people alone.

- Commerce over Conflict: "agreement to disagree.”
    
    - Locke's solution to violence can be summarized in three key steps:
        
        1. **Obscure the Questions**: In a capitalist world, violent debates about religious truth or the nature of virtue are unproductive. They "interfere with the productive conduct of commerce," so it is best to eliminate or obscure them.
        
        1. **Promote Self-Preservation**: The primary human desire is reframed not as the pursuit of glory or religious truth, but as self-preservation and the "pursuit of happiness." A comfortable life is preferable to a heroic but meaningless death.
        
        1. **Unleash Capitalism**: By removing sin from avarice and encouraging the "infinite accumulation of wealth," human energy is redirected. Instead of fighting over God, people are incentivized to engage in productive commerce.
