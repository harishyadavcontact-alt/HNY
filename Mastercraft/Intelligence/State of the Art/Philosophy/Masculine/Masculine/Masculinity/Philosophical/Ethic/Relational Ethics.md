
Taleb’s take on ethics, opportunism, and personal life is grounded in the necessity of **Symmetry** and the distinction between domains where one should be an opportunist versus where one must remain loyal.

### I. Opportunism vs. Loyalty: The Scale-Dependent Rule

- **Opportunism in Business and Intellect:** In the spheres of business and research, one should be a "rational flâneur," maintaining **optionality** to revise schedules and targets as new information arrives.
- **Loyalty in Private Life:** The opportunism of the flâneur is forbidden in personal life and human relations. Here, the noble sentiment is **loyalty**, which must be invested in moral commitments and people.
- **The Conflict:** One cannot apply the logic of a "transactional" life to the "relational" domain of friends and family.

### II. Integration of Private, Personal, and Public Life

- **The Principle of Generalization:** If your private actions do not generalize to your public views, your general ideas are invalid.
- **Consistency of Character:** If your private life conflicts with your intellectual opinion, it cancels the intellectual ideas.
- **Virtue as a Private Act:** Virtue is defined by what you do when nobody is looking; advertising it (virtue signaling) is a vice that combines bullying and cowardice.
- **Ethical Inversion:** It is a modern pathology for people to fit their ethics to their profession (e.g., "I have a mortgage") rather than finding a profession that fits their ethics.

### III. Family and Ethical Vulnerability

- **Family as a Target:** Having a family makes an individual vulnerable to ethical compromise because enemies can hit you where it hurts: the well-being of your progeny.
- **The Celibacy of Saints:** Society traditionally expects moral heroes and saints to be celibate so they can avoid the dilemma of choosing between the collective good and feeding their own children.
- **Marriage and Children:** Marriage is a commitment to children rather than personal happiness; the highest virtue is staving off one's own happiness for the sake of the next generation.
- **The Hierarchy of Survival:** A person is not a single unit; your skin is in a broader set that includes family, tribe, and community. One should be willing to sacrifice their own well-being for the survival of a "higher layer," such as the family or the ecosystem.

### IV. Personal Conduct and Honor

- **Megalopsychon (Grandeur):** Dignity is worth nothing unless you are willing to pay a price for it. Success is defined strictly as leading an **honorable life**, which includes categorical "unconditionals" you would never do for any amount of money.
- **The Social Rule:** Be nice and reasonable to everyone you meet by default, but if someone tries to exercise power over you, exercise power over them.
- **Self-Ownership:** A free person is defined as someone who owns their own opinion and cannot be squeezed into doing something they would otherwise never do.
