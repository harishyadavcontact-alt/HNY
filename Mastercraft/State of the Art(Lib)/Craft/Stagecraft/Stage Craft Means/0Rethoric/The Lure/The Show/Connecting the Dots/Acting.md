# Acting

> Get out of here to get what the character wants.

Strategy  
• Reset your mind to your character’s thoughts and react.  
  
1. **What you think is what you are.**  
2. **the key to authentic and fresh potrayal is thinking your characters thoughts.**  
3. **usually we have self critical and self concious toughts and in order to not fuck up the act, we tend to stop think ing which is fkn nuts and leads to a mindless performance .**  
4. **As humans we never stop thinking and what we think is what we are. The trick is to know your character well enough to know what he or she would think in every situation.**  
5. **The thoughts will create feelings and emotions in the actor and the audience.**  
6. _**All the world’s a stage. And all the men and women merely players”.**_  
7. _**Whether you realize it or not, you are choosing the character you are playing every moment of your life by the thoughts you allow to run through your mind. Who do you want to play in this comedy/drama of your life? It’s your choice.**_  
8. _**You are the star of the show, for sure, but will you play the victim, the loser, the villain or the hero? A lot of it is determined by the thoughts you choose to think.**_  
9. _**If you are thinking thoughts that you wouldn’t say out loud, you need to know that you are projecting their message loudly and clearly, even if your lips aren’t moving.**_  
10. _**The other characters in your story are responding to them as though you were saying them out loud.**_  
11. _**It’s a heaven or hell creating situation.**_**On stage or off, “What you think is what you are”. Get out of the head that is thinking the wrong way by thinking the right way.**  
12. **Use your imagination to think about what kind of character you want to have. Know that character well enough to allow his/her mind to be your mind. Think those thoughts. They will trigger the emotions and feelings that will attract what you want in your performance and in your life.**  
13. **You are doing it, randomly, anyway, when you allow your mind to “go wild”. Might as well play the role you truly want to play.**  
14. **Your character is not thinking about his face. He’s immersed in his current quest. If you are thinking about your face, you will be playing the role of someone trying to make a face.**  
15. **Bottom line, you can’t watch yourself when you are acting. You can’t try to show an audience anything. You cannot aim for a certain effect. Not if you want to give a believable performance. You must simply be involved as your character…completely, naturally and sincerely.**  
16. **If you do that, it all will happen on its own, perfectly…just as it does for you in your real life, every day you are alive.The “goal of the scene” is also not something you would think about as you are performing.**  
17. **Perhaps the writer’s goal is to “scare the audience” and as you are preparing the role it is something to consider as you choose your thoughts and subtext.**  
18. **But that should never enter your mind as you are acting. If the scene’s purpose is to scare the audience, then your job in the scene is to be truly and believably frightened. But as far as you are concerned - AS you are acting, THERE IS NO AUDIENCE. It’s just you and a serial killer.**  
19. **Alone…in your house…with no one nearby to hear your screams.But even when they are not speaking, they are giving you feedback.**  
20. **You never memorize speeches to give to people in real life (I hope). You speak to someone in reaction to something that has happened or has been said. You reply. You will then see a response in their face and body language.**  
21. **They nod or shake their head. You can almost guess what they are about to say and you respond before they do.**  
22. **Then, before they have a chance to say what you know they are going to say next, you have another reply. You may be dominating the conversation, but it is, nevertheless, a conversation. The other person is making you say what you are saying.**  
23. **When you see that a certain tactic isn’t working on them, you change to another one. The other person causes it all. The scene is really not about you. It’s about trying to get them to change.**  
24. **Even if you are doing a soliloquy, alone on stage, you must also create another person to speak to. It is often another part of yourself, or God or an imaginary friend.**  
25. **Occasionally you will change points of view with them…going back and forth between perspectives, playing devils advocate in a discussion. But there is alway an argument…a focus for your intent.**  
26. **Every time someone speaks to us, it triggers a reaction in our minds. We think a thought in response to what they have just said, and that thought leads into what we say. Without the thought/reaction, the spoken line will come out of nowhere.**  
27. **This applies to dialogue that is written in the script as well as to dialogue you make up for your monologue.**  
28. **In order to put that dialogue to use, you must allow yourself to actually be affected by what the other person says. Do you see how if you have nothing to react to, and no thoughts to transition from one idea to the next, this whole thing might become just one run-on sentence?**  
29. **But when you are reacting to the other person and allowing what they say to affect you and make you think, you have a reason to come up with the line you are about to say.**  
30. **Your character yourself**  
31. **so choose wich self are you gonna be.**  
32. **Statements vs responses no statements, please.**  
33. **PAUSING - LISTENING - THINKING. Never stop BEING your character**  
• Who am I?  
  
1. **The better you know the character the better you can poteray him.**  
2. **Every action he does must be justified .**  
• Who am I talking to?  
  
1. **What is the relationship with that person?**  
2. **No matter how much backstory you create for your character , it is actually the person you are speaking to who should be the focus of the scene.**  
3. **Rather than it being about you, the scene should become about them and how you want to change them.**  
4. **Look for a similar relationship in real life , and since every person brings out something different in you Be specific when choosing who you might cast (in your mind) as that other person, from the people in your own life.**  
5. **Suppose you are doing a monologue and you are speaking into the camera or doing a soliloquy….you still should choose a person from your own experience to converse with.**  
• Where am I?  
  
1. **Your environment has a big effect on how you feel and act.**  
2. **So know what time is it.**  
3. **where exactly are you?**  
4. W**hat’s the climate.**  
• What do I want from the person I am speaking to? OBJECTIVE.  
  
1. **Its All about them**  
2. **First you need to know what exactly is going on.**  
3. **figure out WHY you are saying what you are saying**  
4. **It is always this: You want to do something to someone in order to get them to do something. You want to change them. You want them to see things your way. You are in pursuit of a goal. And it’s all about the other person**  
5. **Everything you are saying in the dialogue is your ammunition for getting what you want.**  
6. **As you read through your lines, try to ask yourself “Why am I saying this right now to this person?” Every single thing you say must fit into that one purpose…something you want from the other person. It can’t just be some of your lines…it must be all of them. You want one thing. You want to change the other person in some way.**  
7. **Try to find the best way to describe that desire.**  
8. **Once you have a very clear intention, you will know how to say those lines…the way that will be most convincing with the other person.**  
9. **You will try different tactics for getting what you want. But there is only one desired goal. Your objective.**  
10. **But what might be even more important in deciphering exactly what that is, is what the other character is saying.**  
11. **They are giving you your opposition. What is often missing from a scene when it lacks excitement and energy is a difference of opinion. When two people agree, there is no reason for them to talk at any length. It is when they disagree that a real conversation begins.**  
12. **Each character wants t**  
13. **he other to come over to their side. They are coming from different perspectives. They want different things. So if you look at the other character’s lines and they trigger you into dialogue, you can be fairly sure they are giving you less than (or the opposite of)what you want from them.**  
14. **This should give you some good insight into what it is you do want from them. It is your job to make the scene important. Without opposition there is no scene.**  
15. **If there doesn’t appear to be any conflict, it must be hidden in the subtext. Read between the lines. Imagine what history has culminated in this moment.**  
16. **Find the point of dissension and discern how your character will deal with it.**  
17. **It is often such a temptation to make a scene all about you…to show your inner conflict and the reasons you need what you need. But this always produces a self-indulgent and boring performance because you will be** _**ACTING ALONE.**_  
18. **For you, the focus of the scene should always be on the other person. It’s all about convincing them, changing them, testing them, provoking them, reacting to them and responding to them, It’s never about you. It’s all about them.**  
19. **This is your objective… the big “act” of your ACTing.**  
20. **Every word you say in the scene is to accomplish this…to get the other person to give you what you desire.**  
21. **This quest - changing the other character, is what the scene is all about.**  
22. **Ask yourself: Why do I want it?…Why now?…What will happen if I can’t get it now?,**  
23. **What obstacles are keeping me from it? You have a need that must be fulfilled.**  
24. **This is an important choice you must always make as you prepare and perform your role.**  
• What was the conversation leading to the first line?  
  
1. **Your character didnt sudenly apper out of now where on his first appearance in the story.**  
2. **His entire life led up to that moment.**  
3. **The scene began because something was said or happend before it began.It probably isn’t in the script**  
4. **So you need to know why and how you came to that moment.**  
5. **Your scene is a continuation…a response to what happened before.**  
• What am I really saying?  
  
1. **Your character or as a matter of fact you IRL each time we speak we must choose a limited number of words to communicate so much more.**  
2. **Take a look at each and every one of your lines. There is a different meaning beneath each of them.**  
3. **This is called “subtext” and your performance should be rich with it.**  
4. **What you are thinking as you say something gives it its true meaning.**  
5. **So your thoughts as you speak are far more important than what you are actually saying.**  
6. **it is crucial in creating a deep and meaningful performance.**  
• What is the other character saying?  
  
1. **You are always in dilogue. acting is reacting.**  
2. **You must write the scene as a dilogue so that you Know what you are responding to so every line you say can be an answer.Even when the other character is saying nothing, The other line can just be a look on their face.**  
3. **There is no right responce to each line.**  
4. **Even when the character is saying nothing , you are always responding to what you think they are saying or thinking.**  
5. **when they are saying you are interpreting what theyare saying.**  
6. **Acting is reacting.**

1. **Empathize each word to emphasize it.**  
2. **Scuse the juice out of each word.**

• What are my tactics?  
  
1. **You have one objective but you should employ a varied strategy to accomplish it. As you look through your lines again, explore the different ways you are using your words as you attempt to move the other person to your way of thinking.**  
2. **You may try humor or sympathy, bribes or threats, guilt or anger. This makes for an interesting quest.ways your character attempts to achieve his or her “objective” in the scene.**  
3. **It is a single intention that lasts throughout the entire scene, from beginning to end (unless an event occurs to alter it, like a fire breaking out in the middle of a marriage proposal).**  
4. **The aim is to change the other character from point A to point B. The other character is resisting the change in some way or has the opposite opinion.**  
5. **This is what propels your character through the scene. And the test of whether he achieves his goal is always in the other character.**  
6. **Remember, the scene is never about you. It is about the other person and whether or not you succeed with him or her.**  
7. **When deciding on your objective, it shouldn’t be too easy and it should be something interesting to attempt…something with a definite success or failure by the end of the scene.**  
8. **So your character is on a singular mission to influence someone else. That does not fluctuate. What does change, (and needs to change to make an interesting performance) is the different ways he attempts to make this happen.**  
9. **He/she tries different tactics to get the other character to shift their position.You have failed so far. You need to try something different.**  
10. **Her “no” is what triggers a transition to a new tactic.**  
11. **Now you are employing guilt to get what you want. We might call this the “poor me” tactic.**  
12. **if she had shown any sign that one of the tactics was working you wouldn’t have changed it. You would have played it for all it was worth.**  
13. **It is always the other character that causes you to either continue or alter your strategy.**  
14. **The test is in the other person. she is going to do what ever she can do to make her smile…feel happy.**  
15. **The best tactic for doing this is to be happy herself.And isn’t this exactly what we all do in real life? If you are trying to cheer someone up, what do you do? Don’t you use your own happiness to get them happier?**  
16. **And if you want someone to take something seriously, aren’t you being serious? Its not really about what you are feeling at the time. It’s about what you are trying to DO to the other person.**  
17. **TRANSITIONS - Tactics hey make you THINK a transitional thought: “Better try this then!” This is why it’s so important to write your monologue into a dialogue and have the tactic change written before the line it applies to.**  
18. **If you don’t know what your character is responding to when he/she changes tactics, you can’t have the transitional thoughts your character must think.**  
19. **And if you don’t know a tactic change is coming you can’t do it either. They are all connected. And the other person triggers all of those changes.I**  
20. **t would be great if you could just react spontaneously and naturally as you hear the other person’s lines. But sometimes it takes some consideration as to how your character would react to what is being said.**  
21. **And in the instance of doing a monologue alone, you need to imagine that you hear it, so it’s even harder to be spontaneous. And at the very least, this is a reminder to you all that you must always react…because some actors just don’t.**  
22. **AS your character, you need to know what the other character is saying so you can listen and react to it. But you don’t actually think the other person’s lines. YOU think the reaction thought.**  
23. **So you are always either thinking a reaction thought or a spoken line. You go from one to another…constantly. From reaction thought to spoken thought to reaction thought as you observe and listen to the other person.**
