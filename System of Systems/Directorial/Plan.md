# Indefinitive Optimist

Indefinite Optimism: Optionality

After a brief pessimistic phase in the 1970s, indefinite optimism has dominated

American thinking ever since 1982, when a long bull market began and finance

eclipsed engineering as the way to approach the future.

**To an indefinite optimist,**

**the future will be better, but he doesn’t know how exactly, so he won’t make any**

**specific plans. He expects to profit from the future but sees no reason to design it concretely.**

Instead of working for years to build a new product, indefinite optimists rearrange already-invented ones. Bankers make money by rearranging the capital structures of already existing companies.

Lawyers resolve disputes over old things or help other people structure their affairs. And private equity investors and management consultants don’t start new businesses; they squeeze extra efficiency from old ones with incessant procedural optimizations.

It’s no surprise that these fields all attract disproportionate numbers of high-achieving Ivy League optionality chasers; what could be a more appropriate reward for two decades of résumé-building than a seemingly elite, process-oriented career that promises to “keep options open”?

1. But indefinite optimism seems inherently unsustainable: how can the future get better if no one plans for it?
